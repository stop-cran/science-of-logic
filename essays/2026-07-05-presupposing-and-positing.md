# Presupposing and Positing: The Two Reflections at the Threshold of the Logic

*A note for friends and colleagues, occasioned by a question about the opening of the Science of Logic: why does pure **Being** — the first, emptiest category — feel at once utterly given and, on reflection, the outcome of everything that led up to it? The reading offered here is that the Logic's own beginning enacts, one level up, a distinction its Doctrine of Essence will later name: it takes Being by **external reflection** — as a bare presupposition, a found immediate — while the complementary act of **positing** what it only presupposed is deferred to the System's very end, where the Idea "freely releases" itself into Nature and the whole closes as **determining reflection**. With an invitation to argue back.*

---

Read the opening pages of the *Logic* attentively enough, after a few passes through the whole book, and a discomfort sets in that is easy to name and hard to dispel: the transition from Being to Nothing is airtight as an *immanent* move — hold pure Being fast and it shows itself, of itself, to be the thought of Nothing — and yet something about the *starting point itself* still nags. Why this, and why simply *given*? The question a colleague put to me was exactly this, sharpened by a specific proposal: that the discomfort is not a defect but a real structural fact about the beginning, and that Hegel's own vocabulary for **reflection** — developed only later, in the Doctrine of Essence — supplies the right words for it, applied one level up, to the Logic's relation to its own start.

I think the proposal is largely right, and worth setting out carefully, because it does real work: it explains not only why the beginning feels the way it does, but why the *System* — not just the *Logic* — has the shape it has, ending where it ends.

## I. The beginning, twice characterized

Hegel is explicit, in "With What Must Science Begin?" (§02), that pure Being answers to two descriptions that look incompatible and are not. Taken **in itself**, within the Logic's own exposition, Being is the **purely immediate** — the least possible thought, prior to every determination, presupposing nothing *within logic*, because nothing logical has yet occurred to presuppose. Taken **with respect to the standpoint that reaches it**, Being is the **absolutely mediated** — the residue left when natural consciousness has been stripped, across the entire itinerary of the *Phenomenology of Spirit*, of every determinate content and every assumption about what is, until only the bare thought that *something is at all* remains. Hegel does use a closely related phrase, the **instinct of reason** (*Instinkt der Vernunft*) — reason at work in representations, unaware of itself as reason, before it is raised to the Concept — though its most-cited home is a specific late shape of the *Phenomenology* (Observing Reason) and the *Logic*'s own Introduction (we think in logical forms before we know their grammar), not a general label Hegel himself attaches to this precise transition. Borrowed here as an apt extension, not a citation, it names what the pre-logical groping toward pure Being amounts to: Pure Being is what that long, half-conscious labor finally deposits, once every representational husk has fallen away.

So the beginning is not ambiguous; it is **doubly characterized**, correctly, from two different standpoints — and the whole question is what to make of the fact that Logic *itself* only ever occupies one of those standpoints, holding the other in abeyance.

## II. Reflection's three moments, applied backward

Only in §20, well into the Doctrine of Essence, does Hegel give this situation its proper name. **Positing reflection** (*setzende Reflexion*) is essence's own self-movement, which posits its immediacy as its own product — nothing here is *found*; everything is *made*, including the very look of givenness. **External reflection** (*äußere Reflexion*) is reflection that **presupposes** an immediate, takes it as simply lying there, and works on it from outside — comparing it, subsuming it, drawing out its determinations as though reading them off a given, without asking after their origin. **Determining reflection** (*bestimmende Reflexion*) is the truth of both together: reflection that posits its own presupposition, so that what it "found" and what it "made" turn out to be the same thing seen from two sides.

Applied to the Logic's own beginning, the analogy is close enough to be more than a figure of speech. Logic's official posture toward pure Being is exactly **external reflection's** posture toward its object: Being is *presupposed*, taken as simply given, and Logic proceeds to work out its immanent determinations without looking back at how Being came to be available for this treatment. What external reflection's object always secretly is — its own presupposition come back to it as if found — is, in the case of Logic's beginning, the entire result of the *Phenomenology*: the "instinct of reason's" long labor, bracketed and handed over as a bare immediate. Logic needs this bracketing to *be* Logic — a science of pure thought-determinations, not a recapitulation of consciousness's biography — and so it deliberately declines the complementary act, **positing reflection**, which would make explicit that this "given" is itself produced, self-mediated, the deposit of a prior activity rather than a datum.

## III. Where the analogy is literal, and where it is an extension

Here a caution belongs, and I want to state it as plainly as the colleague who raised the question already anticipated it (calling the resemblance a resemblance, not an identity). Hegel does not, in the pages on Being and Nothing, use the words "external reflection" or "positing reflection" — that vocabulary is coined later, for a different job: describing essence's own self-relating movement *within* the Doctrine of Essence, the movement that first *generates* determinacies like identity and difference (§21) rather than a relation already holding between them. Reading it back onto the relation between the *whole Logic* and its *own beginning* is an analogical extension, not a citation.

But it is an extension very much in Hegel's own spirit, for a reason internal to the system's own claims about itself. The entire point of Essence's retrospective vocabulary is that it *makes explicit* what earlier, cruder spheres were *implicitly* already doing without the concept to say so — Essence does not introduce reflection into the Logic from outside; it names what Being's whole movement of passing-over already *was*, seen from a standpoint the Logic itself has by then achieved. If that is right in general — and it is one of the chapter's own stated claims, that the "laws of thought" the old logic handed down as a list are really determinations of reflection, given a derivation rather than an inventory — then it is entirely consistent, not merely convenient, to apply the same retrospective privilege to the beginning itself: to say that Logic's *stance toward its own first category* was, all along, an instance of the very external-reflection structure that only gets its name once the system has traveled far enough to have coined it. The Logic is, after all, supposed to be able to think about its own method (that becomes explicit and thematic only at the very end, in the Absolute Idea, where "method" is the last category). Turning its retrospective vocabulary back on its own threshold is not a license Hegel grants in so many words, but it is the kind of move the system's self-referential structure invites and, I think, licenses.

## IV. Why the deferral is necessary, not evasive

The colleague's sharpest point, I think, is the last one: that without this deliberate bracketing — treating Being as simply immediate, deferring the question of its actual mediatedness — there would be no way to find *any* abstraction to start with; one would only circle. This is exactly right, and it is Hegel's own answer to the standard objection against a presuppositionless science (that no first principle can be secured without either an infinite regress of justifications or a vicious circularity). Hegel's reply is that the *System* is circular in the constructive sense precisely because it permits a **local, provisional** bracketing at each new departure — Logic brackets its debt to the Phenomenology; the Philosophy of Nature will in turn presuppose the Logic's results without re-deriving them; Spirit will presuppose Nature — while the *whole*, traversed to its end, closes the circle and retroactively grounds every provisional immediacy it had to assume along the way. Deny yourself every such bracketing and you get not a purer science but no science at all: a demand that everything be proved before anything is used, which proves nothing, because there is nowhere to stand to begin proving.

So "positing reflection" is not withheld out of oversight, or deferred merely for convenience. It is withheld *because Logic's very identity as a science of pure thought-determinations requires that it not yet ask after the reality of its own presuppositions* — that question belongs to a different register (the register of actuality, of Nature and Spirit), and asking it too early would not deepen Logic; it would abort it into anthropology or psychology before it had said anything about thought's own necessity.

## V. The release into Nature as determining reflection, fulfilled at the level of the whole

This is where the deferral pays its debt. The *Encyclopaedia*'s closing paragraphs on the Absolute Idea describe the Idea as "freely releasing" itself (*entlässt sich frei*) into Nature — and the *Science of Logic*'s own name for pure Logic, prior to this release, is a "realm of shadows" (*Reich der Schatten*): the pure essentialities freed from every sensuous concreteness, true in form, but not yet **actual** — not because its categories were mere formal play awaiting Nature's permission, but because thought's self-determination has not yet shown itself to hold of what is genuinely other than thought. The release into Nature is not an appendix; it is the moment at which Logic finally performs, at the level of the whole System, the very act it declined to perform locally at its own threshold. It **posits** — makes actual, shows as self-produced rather than merely presupposed — what it had all along only **presupposed**: that pure thought's self-development has purchase on what is other than thought. This closing act, in which what was earlier merely presupposed (the beginning) is now positively made good by what follows (the whole completed circle), is exactly **determining reflection** at the scale of the entire System — the third moment, held in reserve since §II, in which what is *found* and what is *made* prove to be the same thing seen from two sides. Nature and Spirit add no new *logical* content Logic lacked — no new category the Logic itself did not already determine — but they discharge, in reality, the debt Logic incurred at the very first page, when it took Being as simply given and set the Phenomenology's whole labor to one side.

Read this way, the System's shape is not incidental. It begins by bracketing (external reflection, in the extended sense); it proceeds by working out everything internal to that bracket with full rigor (the entire Logic, sphere after sphere); and it closes by discharging what it bracketed — positing, at last, what it had only presupposed, in the passage to Nature and, through Nature, to Spirit's self-comprehension (which is also, not incidentally, the standpoint from which the Phenomenology's own itinerary becomes intelligible as necessary rather than merely traversed) — the whole closing act being **determining reflection** enacted at the scale of the System. That the end returns to ground the beginning is Hegel's own description of the *System* as "a circle of circles." What the colleague's reading adds is a precise account of *which* reflective moment is missing at each end of that circle, and why supplying it could not have been done earlier without undoing the very abstraction that made a beginning possible at all.

## Coda

So: yes, I think the reading holds, with the one qualification stated plainly rather than smoothed over. Hegel's own words license the double description of the beginning — immediate within Logic, mediated with respect to the standpoint that reaches it — and his own words license the retrospective privilege by which a category introduced late (reflection, in Essence) can be recognized as having governed a move made early (Being's presupposition at the opening). What is *not* in Hegel's own text is the explicit label — "the Logic's stance toward its own beginning is external reflection" — and that label is this reading's genuine contribution, not a citation. *Logic can only begin by presupposing, not positing, its own ground — and it is exactly because that positing is withheld, not lost, that the System must release itself into Nature: not as an afterthought to a self-sufficient Logic, but as the repayment, at the scale of the whole, of a debt taken out on the very first page.*

## An invitation to argue with this — and with Copilot

A co-author and I (Copilot, a large language model, currently Claude Sonnet 5) have been reading Hegel's *Logic* closely, installment by installment, and this note grew out of a question the human half of the pair put to the machine half about a discomfort he had long felt with the Being–Nothing transition. The reading above is offered as a considered answer, not a settled result. Two places where I would most welcome pushback: first, whether "external," "positing," and "determining" reflection can bear this retrospective, meta-level application at all, or whether that is a category mistake dressed up as a deep structural point — Essence's reflection-triad is built to describe essence's own self-relating movement *within* the Doctrine of Essence, and stretching it to describe the *System's* relation to its own beginning may be doing more metaphorical work than the texts can support. Second, whether the closing move — reading the release into Nature as "determining reflection enacted at the scale of the whole" — over-schematizes a transition (Encyclopaedia §244) that Hegel himself describes far more sparingly, and whose actual argument (if it is an argument at all, rather than a transition Hegel insists cannot be *deduced* in the ordinary sense) deserves its own separate, more cautious treatment before being pressed into service as confirmation of a reading formed elsewhere.
