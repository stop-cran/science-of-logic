# Quality, Something, and Other

*The second installment of Section III — a close reading of the first internal articulation of* Dasein: *the two sides of its determinacy as **Reality** and **Negation**, their unity as **Quality**, the emergence of the **Something** (*Etwas*) as the first self-related determinate, and the way every Something is at once a Something **and an Other** — with the standard contemporary misreadings of "negation" answered inline.*

---

## I. Where We Are, and Why Dasein Cannot Yet Stand Alone

The previous installment closed with *Dasein* as the result of Becoming's self-cancellation. We had three things to say about it: that its immediacy is *resulted* (it stands only as the residue of a movement); that it carries its negation *internally*, as the determinacy by which it is this rather than that; and that it has, in consequence, an *internal field of articulation* that pure Being and pure Nothing did not have. The promise of the previous installment was that this field — Quality, Reality, Negation — would now be developed.

Before unpacking the field, it is worth saying why *Dasein* cannot rest at the abstract level we left it. Section II §02 framed *Dasein* with the placeholder phrase "a determinate being" or "being-this-rather-than-that," and the previous installment justified that placeholder by showing what the determinacy is and where it comes from. But "being-this-rather-than-that" already contains, in its very phrase, two distinguishable sides: a *being-this* and a *not-being-that*. So far we have used both sides interchangeably, treating the determinacy as a single character of *Dasein*. In fact the two sides are distinguishable, and as soon as we attend to the distinction the dialectical movement resumes.

This is also the first place in the *Logic* at which one can see why Hegel's exposition is **immanent** rather than constructive. He does not introduce Quality, Reality, and Negation as *new* determinations imported from outside; he extracts them from what was already, implicitly, in *Dasein*. Watch the extraction.

## II. Reality and Negation as the Two Sides of Quality

*Dasein* is determinate. To be determinate is to be *thus* and not *otherwise*. The "thus" and the "not otherwise" can be looked at separately.

Look first at the **affirmative** side: *Dasein* simply has its determinacy as a positive character. It is what it is, with this character. Hegel calls this side **Reality** (*Realität*). Reality is not, here, "the totality of what exists" or "the world out there" — these are richer, derivative meanings of the word; the *Logic* uses "Reality" in its strict and minimal sense: the affirmative aspect of a determinacy, the *being-thus* of a being-thus-and-not-otherwise.

Look next at the **negative** side: the very same determinacy is also a *not-being-otherwise*. The character is what it is by being not-the-other. Hegel calls this side **Negation** (*Negation*). Negation here is again to be taken strictly: not the operator of formal logic, not the gesture of denial, not nothingness, but the negative aspect of a determinacy — the *not-being-otherwise* of a being-thus-and-not-otherwise.

These two sides — Reality and Negation — are not two ingredients out of which the determinacy is composed; they are the same determinacy looked at from two sides. There is no Reality (in the strict sense) without Negation (in the strict sense), and vice versa. A "thus" without a "not otherwise" would be no determinacy at all (it would be back to the indistinct *is* of pure Being); a "not otherwise" without a "thus" would be no determinacy either (it would be back to pure Nothing). The two sides require each other.

Their unity is **Quality** (*Qualität*). Quality is not a third thing alongside Reality and Negation; it is precisely their unity in the determinacy itself. To say of a *Dasein* that it has a quality is to say that it has a determinate character — affirmative on one side, negative on the other, both at once.

## III. Anticipating the Standard Misreadings

This is the place where the *Logic* most often loses contemporary readers — and where it most often gets attacked as confused. Three misreadings are worth confronting directly.

The first is the **representational** misreading: that "Reality" must mean "what really exists out there," "Negation" must mean "what does not exist," and Hegel is therefore making a strange claim about how non-existent things contribute to existent ones. The misreading is understandable, since "reality" in everyday and even much philosophical English carries the meaning of "the totality of what is the case." But the *Logic* is not, at this stage, doing ontology in that sense; it is articulating the structure of *determinacy*. "Reality" in the *Logic* is a *moment* of any determinacy whatever, including the determinacy of, say, a triangle or a melody — it does not mean "the existing world." Once this is granted, the strangeness vanishes: of course every determinate thing is what it is partly *by being not-other-things*, and that is all "Negation" amounts to here.

The second is the **logical-positivist** misreading: that "Negation" in Hegel must be the formal operator ¬, and Hegel's claims about Negation are claims about ¬ — claims which are then either trivial or false. The misreading is again understandable, because the formal operator is now so familiar that "negation" is hard to hear in any other sense. But the formal operator presupposes a logic in which there are propositions that can be denied, and that logic is much later in the order of exposition than the *Logic*'s own order. At the level of *Dasein*, there are not yet propositions; there are only determinacies. The Negation that is at issue is not the operator on propositions but the *not-being-otherwise* internal to any determinacy. The formal operator ¬ is itself, much later, one of the things the *Logic* explains — it is the rigid, abstract, finite-understanding shadow of dialectical Negation.

The third is the **mere-difference** misreading: that "Negation" is just a fancy word for "difference," and Hegel's whole machinery here is a heavy-handed way of saying things differ. This misreading underestimates the structure being claimed. To say that *Dasein* contains Negation is not to say that one *Dasein* differs from another (that would be an external comparison). It is to say that the *being-this* of any one *Dasein* is, in itself, *not-being-other* — that the negative reference is *internal* to the determinacy, not external between determinacies. Difference is between things; the Hegelian Negation is *within* a thing, as the very structure of its determinacy. This will become crucial in the next move.

A fourth temptation, less an explicit misreading than a habit of reading, is to treat Reality and Negation as *symmetric* — as if the determinacy were equally well grasped from either side and there were no asymmetry between them. This is almost right but not quite. They are equally indispensable, but they are *opposed* sides of one determinacy, and the very fact that they are opposed sides of *one* determinacy is what will drive the next step. The single determinacy contains an opposition within itself — and an opposition contained within a unity is, as we already know from Becoming, an unstable structure. *Dasein*, taken with its quality, is therefore already restless.

## IV. From Quality to Something — the First Negation of the Negation

Hold the structure firmly. *Dasein* has a quality; quality is the unity of Reality and Negation. The two are sides of one determinacy and require each other. So far so static — but only if we stop here. As soon as we attend to what we have, the movement resumes.

Notice: the Negation in *Dasein* is *not* an external negation that some other being supplies. It is the very determinacy of *Dasein* itself, taken from its negative side. So Negation, here, is *internal* to *Dasein* — the *Dasein*'s own *not-being-otherwise*.

Now apply Negation again. The Negation in *Dasein* is *itself* one side of its determinacy, opposed to the other side (Reality). To negate the Negation — that is, to attend to the side of *Dasein* that is not its negative side, but the positive side that contains the negative as its own moment — is to arrive at *Dasein* not as a mere this-or-that determinacy spread out between Reality and Negation, but as a *self-related unity* that holds both sides together.

Hegel calls this self-related unity **Something** (*Etwas*).

Something is the *first negation of the negation* in the *Logic*. Pure Being collapsed into Nothing — that was the first negation. Becoming collapsed and left a determinate residue — that brought Negation inside as a moment of *Dasein*. Now this internal Negation is itself negated — not by being denied or removed, but by being *taken up into* a unity that includes it as one of its sides. The result is no longer just "a determinacy" but "a determinate that is *one*, that holds together as itself."

The phrase "negation of the negation" can sound formulaic — and there is a long history of objections to it (Dühring's, famously, and a thousand others) that treat it as a slogan. But here, watched closely, it is not a slogan; it is the description of a definite structural move. The first negation produced an *internal opposition* within the determinacy. The second negation lets that opposition be recognized as an opposition *within a unity* — and the unity, recognized as such, is a new determination. This is not magic, and it is not double-counting; it is the bare structure of "this determinacy is *one* even though it has two sides."

A useful contrast: where mere *Dasein* could be described impersonally as "a being-this-rather-than-that," the **Something** must be described with the indefinite article and a self-reference: it is *a* something — *this* one — held together as itself. The grammatical shift is not accidental. *Dasein* could still, taken thinly, look like just an instance of a quality; **Something** is a *bearer* of its quality, an *it* that *has* the quality. We have moved from determinacy-in-general to a *determinate that is a one*.

## V. Something Is Not Yet an "Object"

A common stumble at this point is to think that we have just smuggled in the rich notion of "an object" — perhaps even a substance, in the heavy ontological sense — under the modest title "Something." We have not, and it is worth seeing why.

The Something of the *Logic* is, at this stage, the *minimum* of self-relation: the bare unity by which a determinacy holds together as one. It has no further structure. It does not yet have *parts*. It does not yet have *properties* in the predicational sense — that comes much later, in Essence. It does not yet have *identity through change* — that comes through the development of finitude and reciprocity with the Other. It does not yet have any internal differentiation between its essence and its appearance — Essence as a sphere is still far ahead. All it has is this: that its quality, with its Reality-side and its Negation-side, is held together as belonging to *one*.

Equally, Something is not yet a *subject* in any sense richer than this minimal self-relation. We are still in the Doctrine of Being. Subjectivity in the strong Hegelian sense — the sphere of the Concept, in which a determination relates to itself by relating to its other and remains itself in the relation — is the destination of the *Logic*, not its second move. Something is the seed of that destination, not its arrival.

But Something is also genuinely more than *Dasein*. The difference is that *Dasein* could be characterized purely "from outside," as a determinacy spread between two sides; **Something** must be characterized with the structural fact that the two sides are *its* sides. The "its" is the new content. Without the "its," there is no Something; only the "its" makes the negation-of-the-negation a unity rather than just an oscillation.

The reader who has followed the move feels, at this point, that we have arrived at something firmer than what we had before. The feeling is correct. We have, for the first time in the *Logic*, a determination that has *self-reference*. It does not yet have a *self* in any rich sense — but it has the structure that all later self-relations will refine and deepen. From this point on, the *Logic*'s movement consists in articulating what it takes for a determination to be self-related.

## VI. Why Something Cannot Stand Alone — the Other

Hold Something firmly. It is *one*, it is *this*, its quality holds together as belonging to it. What more can be said?

This: Something is *this* only by being *not-that*. The Negation that was internal to *Dasein* and was sublated in the unity of Something has not vanished; it has been retained as the Something's *being-this-and-not-other*. So the *not-other* is itself a moment of the Something — and a *not-other* is not a feature of nothing in particular; it is a relation to *the other*.

Hence: the Something has, internal to its very being-something, a reference to **another** — to that-which-it-is-not. Hegel calls this other the **Other** (*das Andere*).

The first thing to register is that the Other is not *added to* Something from the outside, as if we were now considering a *second* something next to the first. The Other is, at this stage, *generated by* the Something itself, as the necessary correlate of the Something's *not-being-other*. To be a Something — *any* Something — is already to be related, intrinsically, to an Other. The reference is internal, not contingent.

The second thing to register is that this is symmetric. The Other, taken as itself a Something (and as soon as we attend to it as a determinate it *is* another Something), is in turn related to *its* Other — and that Other turns out to be the original Something we started with. Each is the other's Other; each is a Something only by reference to the other. Hegel formulates this with the (initially baffling) line that "*the other taken for itself is the other within itself*" — meaning that the determination *Other*, considered in itself, is just "that which is what-it-is by being not-the-first," which is precisely the structure of the original Something, only with the labels swapped.

This is a more delicate move than it sounds, and the standard misreadings around it are worth pre-empting.

The first standard misreading is that Hegel is here making a claim about the *external* world — that "everything is related to everything else," or that "no thing exists in isolation," in some vague holistic sense. He is not. The claim is more limited and more precise: the determination *Something*, taken at its level in the *Logic*, is *constitutively* related to the determination *Other*, in the same way and for the same reasons that *Dasein* was constitutively the unity of Reality and Negation. It is a claim about the structure of the determination, not yet a claim about how things in the world hang together.

The second standard misreading is that the move from Something to Other is a *transition* in time, as if we first had a Something and then later acquired an Other. There is no time here; we are at the level of pure determinations. The Other is not what comes *after* the Something; it is what is implied *in* the Something the moment we attend to its *not-being-other*. The two arise in the same logical breath.

The third standard misreading is that the Other must be a *specific* other — that there must be one definite other thing that any given Something is related to. Not yet. At this level, the Other is just *the Other*, indefinitely — the negative correlate of the Something. Specification of the Other (this stone rather than that planet, this number rather than that line) belongs to determinate empirical content, not to the bare logical structure under examination.

What we have, then, is a pair: Something — Other. Each requires the other; each is what it is by being not-the-other; and each, taken in itself, has the same structure as the other. The pair is *reciprocal*.

## VII. The Reciprocity, and What It Forces Next

Stay with the reciprocity. Something is what it is by *not* being the Other; the Other is what it is by *not* being the Something. Each is itself only through its negative reference to the other. Their entire determinacy is in this reciprocal *not-each-other*.

But now: if the Something's *being-this* is *constitutively* its *not-being-the-other*, then a strange thing happens when we ask what Something *is* in itself — what it is *apart from* the relation to the Other. The answer the move forces is: nothing yet. So far, the Something is *exhaustively* described as "that which is not the Other"; subtract the relation to the Other and there is no determinacy left to call "Something." But this seems wrong — we wanted Something to be a *self-related unity*, an *it that has* its quality, not merely a placeholder defined by what it is not.

The contradiction is real, and it is the engine of the next move. *Either* the Something is just-a-not-the-other (in which case its self-relation is empty, and we have not gained anything over *Dasein* taken externally), *or* the Something has a determinacy that is *its own* independently of the relation to the Other (in which case we owe an account of what such a "its own" amounts to). The two sides cannot both be denied, and they cannot both be affirmed without distinction.

Hegel's resolution — which the next installment will develop in full — is that **both sides must be affirmed, but distinguished into two aspects of the Something itself**. The Something has a side by which it *is what it is in itself, in distinction from its relation to the Other* — and a side by which it *is what it is for the Other, in the relation*. These two aspects Hegel calls **being-in-itself** (*Ansichsein*) and **being-for-other** (*Sein-für-Anderes*). They are not two parts of the Something stuck together; they are two sides of the same Something, just as Reality and Negation were two sides of the one quality of *Dasein*.

The same dialectical pattern is recurring at one level higher. *Dasein*'s determinacy was Reality-and-Negation; Something's structure will turn out to be being-in-itself-and-being-for-other. The pattern is not a coincidence. Each time a determination is shown to require a unity that holds together opposed sides, the next move articulates *what those sides are at this new level*. We will meet the pattern again and again. From now on it can be expected — and the expectation is itself a sign that the reader has begun to inhabit the *Logic* rather than merely read it.

## VIII. A Word on Method: What This Step Has Made Visible

Before turning to the next installment, three observations about what has happened in this one are worth registering — observations that will hold, mutatis mutandis, at every later step.

The first is that **each new category was extracted, not added**. Reality and Negation were not introduced as new ingredients; they were the two sides of *Dasein*'s determinacy seen as distinguishable. Quality was not a third thing alongside them; it was their unity. Something was not an ontological inflation of *Dasein*; it was *Dasein* taken as the negation of its own internal Negation. The Other was not a second thing brought in from outside; it was the implicit correlate of the Something's *not-being-other*. At every step, what was already implicit was made explicit. This is what *immanent development* means in the *Logic*, and it is what guards the procedure from the charge of arbitrariness.

The second is that **the misreadings cluster around the same kind of mistake**: importing into the *Logic* a richer category than the *Logic* has yet earned. "Reality" gets misread as "the existing world" because the reader imports the rich Essence-level meaning. "Negation" gets misread as the formal operator ¬ because the reader imports propositional logic. "Something" gets misread as "an object" or "a substance" because the reader imports the categories of the Doctrine of Essence. "Other" gets misread as "another existing thing" because the reader imports the empirical sense of the word. The corrective in every case is the same: read the determination at the level the *Logic* has earned it, and refuse the temptation to fill it in with content that belongs to a later level.

The third is that **what gives the development its compulsion is the internal opposition of any unity that is more than empty**. *Dasein* was a unity of opposed sides (Reality and Negation), and it could not stand still. Something is again a unity of opposed sides — at the new level, the side toward itself and the side toward the Other — and it likewise will not stand still. The *Logic* is not driven by Hegel's wish for it to move; it is driven by the structural fact that any determinate unity contains opposed sides, and opposed sides within a unity are, as Becoming first showed us, restless. To follow the *Logic* is to follow that restlessness wherever it leads.

## IX. What Comes Next

The Something stands now as the correlate of an Other, with the contradiction noted: it is, on the one hand, exhaustively described by its *not-being-the-other*, and yet, on the other hand, must have a determinacy that is *its own* if "Something" is to mean more than "place-holder for the not-other." The next installment will develop the resolution: **being-in-itself** and **being-for-other** as the two sides of the Something at this level; the Something's **determination** (*Bestimmung*) and its **constitution** (*Beschaffenheit*); and the **limit** (*Grenze*) at which the Something both is and is not itself. From the limit, the move to **finitude** — the Something as that which has its end *in itself*, which is the entry into the long arc that will close the first major sub-circle of the Doctrine of Being in the determination of **infinity**.

---

## Coda

A reader who has followed this installment may notice that the pace of the *Logic* has begun to tell its own story. From pure Being to *Dasein* the work was largely demolition: each thinly held determination collapsed under its own emptiness, and the residue had to be picked up. From *Dasein* onward, the work shifts: each determination is now rich enough to *articulate* itself rather than just collapse, and the next determination emerges by tracing the articulation rather than by registering a collapse. This is not a change of method — it is the same imminent development throughout — but it is a change of *texture*, and it is worth feeling as a change. The *Logic* will become more and more articulated as it proceeds, and the reader who has felt the texture shift here will be ready to feel it shift again at the major junctures ahead.
