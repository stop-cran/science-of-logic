# From School-Logic to Dialectic

*A synopsis of Hegel's account of the historical-logical itinerary by which thought arrives at a method adequate to its content.*

---

## I. The Standpoint of Traditional ("School") Logic

The school-logic of the textbook tradition — the syllogistic apparatus inherited from Aristotle through scholasticism, formalized in the handbooks of Wolff, in Kant's *Logic*, in Mill, and in every introductory textbook to this day — presents itself as the science of correct thinking. Its content is the forms of thought: concept, judgment, syllogism; the laws of identity, of non-contradiction, of the excluded middle.

The striking contradiction is this: the **content** of this logic is *thinking itself*, yet its **method** is utterly **non-thinking**. It is an external, taxonomic enumeration. Logic *says* it studies the laws of thought, yet it presents these laws in the manner of a botanist cataloguing dried specimens. The forms are picked up one after another, classified, and laid side by side as if they were stones in a museum case — each indifferent to the next. Concept, judgment, syllogism appear as three separate "topics" connected by nothing more than the alphabet of the chapters.

This is the scandal: **a science whose subject-matter is the self-movement of thought presents itself in a form that is dead, motionless, and externally arranged.** The very logic that forbids contradiction is self-contradictory in its constitution — it treats the living as a corpse. It can describe a syllogism but cannot show *why* judgment must pass over into syllogism, why "S is P" cannot rest in itself but generates the mediation of a middle term. It enumerates the laws of identity and contradiction but cannot reflect on its *own* identity with — or contradiction to — its object.

Hence school-logic is, in Hegel's phrase, a *formal* logic: form severed from content, and therefore — paradoxically — a content (the table of forms) severed from any form of its own (any internal necessity in their sequence). It is the very type of what Hegel calls *external reflection*: a thinking that stands over against its object instead of *being* the object's own articulation.

This contradiction is not a defect to be patched. It is the symptom that drives thought, in history as in logic, beyond this standpoint. To see why, we must trace the stages by which thinking has actually come to itself.

---

## II. The Historical-Logical Itinerary

Hegel insists: the stages through which thinking has appeared in history are not accidents of culture but **moments** that thought had to traverse. The historical sequence is the temporal display of a logical necessity. Each shape arises because the preceding one, pursued to its limit, generates a contradiction it cannot itself resolve; and each is *aufgehoben* — annulled, preserved, and lifted up — in its successor.

We trace five such moments: pre-logical (representational) consciousness, old metaphysics, empiricism, critical philosophy, and dialectics.

### 1. Pre-logical (Representational) Consciousness

**Achievement.** Thought first awakens in the form of *Vorstellung* — picture-thinking, myth, religion in its representational mode, the immediate certainty of the senses. The world is *there*, peopled with gods, things, qualities. Consciousness here makes its first and indispensable acquisition: the **distinction between itself and an object**, and the conviction that the object is intelligible — that there is a "this" to be grasped.

**Contradiction.** The content is held in the form of the image, the story, the felt certainty. But image and story cannot account for themselves. The "this" of sense-certainty, when one tries to *say* it, immediately becomes a universal ("now," "here," "I") that contradicts the singular it meant to fix. Representation carries truth, but in a form inadequate to truth — in the form of mere given-ness. To name a thing is already to lift it out of its singular here-and-now into a universal; the form of language betrays the form of the meaning.

**Resolution.** Consciousness is driven from the immediate to the mediated, from the image to the **thought-determination** (*Gedankenbestimmung*). It begins to ask not "what is shown?" but "what is it, in itself?" — and so passes into metaphysics.

**What is preserved.** The trust that the object *is* knowable; that thinking and being meet; the indelible character of all subsequent thought as *thought of something*.

### 2. Old (Pre-Kantian) Metaphysics

**Achievement.** This is the standpoint of Parmenides through Wolff: the great proposition that **thought-determinations are determinations of the thing itself**. What reason finds in the concept is what *is*. Old metaphysics produces the categorial vocabulary by which any subsequent science must speak — substance, cause, necessity, infinity, soul, world, God — and treats these as objective predicates of the absolute. It is the first and decisive break with mere representation: it dares to think the unconditioned in concepts.

**Contradiction.** Its method, however, is the same dogmatic enumeration that infects school-logic. It picks up its categories ready-made, uses them in fixed opposition (finite *or* infinite, simple *or* composite, free *or* determined), and assumes that one of each pair must simply be true of the object. Hence the **antinomies**: with equal cogency one proves that the world is finite and that it is infinite, that the soul is simple and that it is composite, that there is a first cause and that the chain of causes regresses without end. The categories, taken as fixed and mutually exclusive, contradict themselves the moment they are applied to the unconditioned. Metaphysics has the courage to claim that thought reaches being, but it lacks the means to show *how*; it asserts the identity of thought and being instead of producing it.

**Resolution.** Two responses become historically necessary, and both follow.

- (a) If thought's fixed categories shipwreck on the object, then thought should *abandon* its a priori pretensions and turn to **experience** — empiricism.
- (b) Or thought should turn back upon *itself* and **critique** its own faculty — critical philosophy.

**What is preserved.** The conviction that thinking grasps the thing; and the categorial inventory itself — substance, cause, ground, force — which never disappears, only changes its status.

### 3. Empiricism

**Achievement.** Empiricism (Bacon, Locke, Hume) makes a decisive advance: it demands that the content of thought be **earned**, not assumed. The principle "nothing in the intellect that was not first in the senses" enthrones perception, observation, experiment. With this comes the birth of the **modern positive sciences** — content of unprecedented richness, lawful regularities, the disciplined humility of *evidence*. Empiricism teaches thought to be *concrete*: to refuse the empty word, to demand the thing. In this it accomplishes what metaphysics could not: it fills thought with content.

**Contradiction.** Yet empiricism, while loudly disclaiming metaphysics, is forced to use metaphysical categories at every step — "thing," "force," "cause," "law," "matter." None of these is given to the senses; each is a thought-determination smuggled in under the table. Hume sees this with terrible clarity: causality, pursued empirically, dissolves into mere customary succession; the necessary connection on which all science rests is **not in the data**. Empiricism thus undermines the very science it sought to ground. Universality and necessity — without which there is no knowledge — cannot be wrung from the singular and the contingent. The principle of empiricism, consistently applied, refutes empiricism.

**Resolution.** If necessity and universality are not in the object as given, they must come from the **subject**. The question is no longer "what is the object?" but "what must the knowing subject contribute so that there be an object at all?" Empiricism, by exhausting the side of the given, drives thought into the critical turn.

**What is preserved.** The demand that thought be filled with content; the rigor of evidence; the whole edifice of the empirical sciences — now to be set on a different foundation.

### 4. Critical Philosophy (Kant)

**Achievement.** Kant's Copernican revolution is the moment at which thought becomes **self-conscious about its own categories**. The forms of intuition (space, time) and the categories of the understanding (substance, cause, reciprocity, etc.) are recognized as the *a priori* contributions of the subject — the conditions under which any object can appear at all. With this, the antinomies of old metaphysics are diagnosed: they arise when the categories, valid only of appearances, are illegitimately applied to the unconditioned. Kant unifies the achievements of the prior two stages: empiricism's content (intuition) and metaphysics' categories (concept) — *"thoughts without content are empty, intuitions without concepts are blind."* He establishes the indispensable principle: **the object of knowledge is constituted in the act of knowing it.** Necessity and universality, denied to empiricism, are won back as the form of cognition itself.

**Contradiction.** But Kant's victory is bought at a ruinous price.

- The categories, because they are *ours*, are declared incapable of reaching **the thing in itself**. Knowledge is hemmed within appearance; the *Ding an sich* lies forever beyond. Yet to say "there *is* a thing in itself, but we cannot know it" is already to apply the categories of *being* and *causality* (it is, it affects us) to that very thing — the categories Kant has just confined to appearance. The doctrine refutes itself in its statement.
- The categories themselves are presented as a **finished table**, picked up, like the school-logic's forms, from the existing logic of judgment — *enumerated, not deduced*. Kant asks the right question (whence the categories?) but answers it externally.
- The antinomies are marked merely as forbidden territory — as a warning sign at the limits of reason — rather than as the **positive engine** of thought.

Critical philosophy thus reproduces, on a higher plane, the very defect of school-logic: it has the categories, but it has them *externally*. It knows that the categories are forms of thought, but it does not know thought as the self-articulation of the categories.

**Resolution.** If the contradictions reason discovers in the unconditioned are not signs of reason's incompetence but of **the very life of the matter** — if the categories are not a finished list but generate one another by their own insufficiency — then critical philosophy passes into **dialectics**. The "thing in itself" is dissolved: there is no beyond to thought, because thought has been shown to *be* the activity in which being articulates itself. The opposition of subject and object, the last residue of representation, is itself comprehended as a moment.

**What is preserved.** The entire critical achievement — that the categories are forms of thought, that the object is constituted in cognition, that intuition and concept must be unified, and the table of categories itself, now to be re-derived from within.

### 5. Dialectics (the Speculative Standpoint)

**Achievement.** Dialectical or speculative thought is the standpoint at which logic at last attains a **method adequate to its content**. The forms of thought are not picked up from outside; each is shown to arise from the immanent self-movement of its predecessor. *Being*, taken purely, shows itself indistinguishable from *Nothing*; their truth is *Becoming*; Becoming determines itself into *Determinate Being*; and so on, through quality, quantity, measure, essence, the concept, the idea — the entire system of categories deduced as a single self-developing whole. Contradiction is no longer a sign of error to be avoided but the **principle of all movement and life** — the very pulse by which one determination passes into another. The categories are **deduced** in their necessary order; the table is replaced by a self-developing system.

By this, three things are accomplished at once:

- **Old metaphysics is rehabilitated**: thought-determinations are again determinations of the thing — but now because subject and object have been shown to share a single conceptual life, not by dogmatic assumption.
- **Empiricism is vindicated**: the concrete content is essential; the Idea is concrete or it is nothing — but this content is no longer the merely given, it is the determinate self-articulation of the universal.
- **Criticism is fulfilled**: the categories are forms of thought, but precisely *because* they are forms of thought they are forms of being — there is no thing-in-itself behind them, because the antithesis of "for us" and "in itself" is itself a posited and overcome moment.

**The contribution to the scientific paradigm.** Where the school-logic offered scientists a static toolbox — a set of rules of inference indifferent to what is inferred about — dialectic offers science its **own self-understanding**: that every concept it employs is finite, generates its own opposite under pressure of its content, and is preserved in a richer concept that mediates the two. Science's history — the passage from mechanism to chemism to organism, from substance to function, from particle to field, from force to energy to information — is no longer a series of accidents but the visible logic of cognition itself working out its determinations on its object. The historical and the logical coincide, because both are the unfolding of one matter: the self-determination of the concept.

The scientific method, on this view, is not a set of external rules imposed on an inert subject-matter (Mill's canons, Popper's falsifiability, the hypothetico-deductive schema), but the **immanent logic of the matter under investigation**, as that logic is reconstructed in thought. Method is not prior to content; it is content come to consciousness of itself.

## Coda: The Ground of What Follows

We have traced the *outer* itinerary — the historical-logical pre-history of speculative science. What remains is to descend into its *inner* movement: to follow the actual self-development of the categories from the simplest determination (*Being*) through their entire articulation, and to exhibit how this movement is at once the structure of thought and the structure of the real.

The next installment will take up the question with which Hegel's *Science of Logic* itself begins: **"With what must science begin?"** — and will show why the answer to that question is already the first step of the method.
