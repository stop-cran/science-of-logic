# From Pure Being to Determinate Being

*The first installment of Section III — a close reading of the opening of the* Science of Logic, *re-traversing at full resolution the move from Being and Nothing through Becoming to* Dasein, *and showing why the abstract* Dasein *genuinely **is** — why it plays on its own field rather than reducing to pure Being all over again.*

---

## Section III: What This Section Does

Section II gave the *Logic* its first traversal — the bird's-eye picture of the entire circle, in which each sphere was characterized by its dominant mode of transition (passing-over in Being, shining-into in Essence, developing-out-of in Concept) and the major dialectical arc was sketched. That traversal earned the right to call the *Logic* a single self-developing whole. But it bought that right at a cost: dozens of times we said things like "and once Becoming has settled into Determinate Being, we have a *something*," compressing into a half-sentence a movement whose internal articulation occupies pages of Hegel's own text. The IOUs flagged at the close of Section II (the question of the calculus, the substance-to-Concept transition, the syllogistic forms, the self-articulation of the Absolute Idea, the alienation question) are the most conspicuous of these compressions — but the entire Section II is a sequence of such compressions, hardly any of which were fully unfolded.

Section III is the deeper traversal, in which we slow down to the pace of the actual moves. We do not aim at encyclopedic completeness — Hegel's own Greater Logic remains the close work, and no synopsis can substitute for it — but we do aim at a fidelity that Section II deliberately did not aim at: every "must" in the text earned in the text, every "passes over" exhibited as a passage, every "sublated" displayed as what it positively retains and what it negatively cancels.

The pay-off is not just better understanding of Hegel. It is something the reader can carry away as a practice: the habit of refusing the satisfaction of having "understood" a determination until its immanent contradiction has been articulated, and the converse habit of trusting that an articulated contradiction is not a failure of thought but its productive form. Once that habit is acquired in the pure logical case, where the categories have no empirical material to distract, it can be turned on any subject-matter whatever.

A word about what Section III is *not*. It is not a commentary on Hegel — not an exegesis whose office would be to track the surface of the text passage by passage and adjudicate the secondary literature. The aim is rather to **do** the close reading, in our own words, with attention to what Hegel actually argues and to the contemporary reader who is more likely to misunderstand the argument by importing one of two opposed bad habits: either a logical-positivist suspicion that the moves are wordplay, or a hermeneutic indulgence that takes the wordplay to be the moves. Each major step will be developed with anticipation of the most common confusions; Hegelian terminology will be used and explained but not multiplied; the German will appear where it earns its keep.

We begin where the *Logic* begins, and where Section II §02 began schematically. What follows is a re-traversal of that opening at the resolution Section III requires.

## I. Why Begin Again

A reader who has worked through Section II might reasonably ask: have we not already done this? Synopsis 02 gave the move from Being through Nothing and Becoming to Dasein, and §VI of that synopsis explicitly distinguished the genuine dialectical step from its schoolroom caricature. What more is there to do?

The answer is that Section II gave the **schema** of the step. It said: there is an immediate determination, it generates its opposite by its own internal demand, and the two are sublated in a third that contains them as moments. It even said this for the specific case of Being / Nothing / Becoming. But a schema is not yet the experience of the step from inside. The reader who follows the schema is in the position of someone who has been told a proof exists and shown the conclusion, but has not watched the proof unfold. The proof here is not an item one possesses by registering it; it is something one possesses by re-tracing.

Hegel's *Logic* is in this respect like a piece of music. Synopsis 02 was the analytical summary of the opening movement: themes, key, structure, where it modulates, where it returns. Section III is the playing of the music. Both are needed, but they are not interchangeable, and one cannot be reduced to the other.

What is in question is also more than narrowly philosophical. The *Logic* purports to be the science of the very forms in which anything whatever can be thought. If those forms are indeed identical with the forms in which anything whatever can **be** — Hegel's deepest claim — then to follow the *Logic* slowly is to acquire, at the level of pure thought, the discipline that mature science is supposed to embody at the level of its concrete content. The reader who has done this work has earned the right to suspect every fixed determination in every science.

## II. Pure Being, Once More — and What It Costs

Take the determination *Being*, with absolute strictness. Strip away every quality, every distinction, every "such-and-such," every "thus-and-so." Let it be only this: that there is something to be thought at all — *is*, taken purely, without specification of what is.

It is essential at this point to resist three opposed temptations.

The first is the temptation to think that pure Being is *some* particular thing or kind of thing — the totality of beings, the supreme being, the substratum of all that is, raw existence as opposed to essence. Each of these is a determinate thought, with its own internal structure, and bringing any of them into the beginning is bringing a content. Pure Being, as Hegel uses the term, is none of them. It is the bare thought of *is*-ness, with every further specification withheld.

The second temptation is the opposite: to think that pure Being is just an empty word, and that emptiness is a defect that disqualifies it as a beginning. "If your beginning is empty," the objection runs, "then it cannot ground anything." But this objection mistakes what is being asked of the beginning. The beginning of a *science of all determinations* cannot itself be a determination — for then it would presuppose what it must produce. The emptiness of Being is not a failure to be a beginning; it is the only thing a genuine beginning could be. Anything richer is borrowed, and anything thinner is nothing at all.

The third temptation, subtler, is to think that pure Being is a *presupposition* — that one has to assume "thought can think being" in order to start. But the beginning is not the *assumption* that thought thinks being; it is the **act** of letting precisely what remains stand, after every assumption has been withdrawn. Even the assumption "thought can think being" is, strictly, a *content* — a relation between thought and being — and so cannot be smuggled in at the beginning. What remains, at the limit of all such withdrawals, is the bare thought *is* itself, without any specification of what does the thinking, of what is thought, or of how. To stand at this point requires nothing to be assumed; it requires only that one allow the residue.

This is, as Section II §02 already noted, why Hegel can say in the same breath that the beginning is the most immediate (taken in itself) and the most mediated (taken with respect to the standpoint that has been won). The whole work of the *Phenomenology of Spirit* — and, more modestly, the work of Sections I and II of this synopsis — is the labor by which natural consciousness is brought to a point at which it can let pure Being stand. Here we simply take that standpoint as already won. What concerns us now is what happens when, having reached it, we attend to pure Being itself.

## III. Pure Nothing, and Why It Is Pure Being

Attend to pure Being. Try to think it.

Notice immediately what happens. There is nothing to attend to. No quality to seize, no relation to traverse, no determination on which to fix. The thought has no internal structure to inhabit. To think pure Being is to be in possession of a thought that, when one looks for what one is thinking, finds — nothing.

This is the first move, and it is essential not to misdescribe it. The argument is *not*: "Being is empty; Nothing is empty; therefore Being and Nothing are the same." That would be external comparison — measuring two thoughts against each other on the criterion of their content. Hegel does not measure; he reports what happens *within* the attempt to think pure Being. What happens is that the attempt collapses into the thought of pure Nothing. Not that pure Being *means* nothing in the sense of being meaningless, but that pure Being, when thought, **is** the very thought of Nothing.

The same in reverse. Attempt to think pure Nothing. Not a particular absence (the absence of this, the absence of that — those are already determinate, and so already richer than the beginning), but pure indeterminacy as such, total emptiness. To think this — actually to think it, not merely to entertain the word — is, again, to be in possession of a thought with no internal structure, no content, no determination. And the very *having* of such a thought, the *that*-it-is-a-thought-at-all, is the bare *is*. Pure Nothing, attended to, is pure Being.

A common objection at this point is that Hegel is exploiting an equivocation in the word "is." There is, the objection runs, a difference between *is* in the sense of *exists* and *is* in the sense of *is identical with*; Hegel slides between them and so produces a paradox that is really a confusion. The objection has a long history (Russell-style readings of Hegel often turn on some version of it) and is worth answering.

The answer is that the *Logic* is not making a claim about word-uses. It is taking the bare *is*-ness — what survives every withdrawal of determination, including the determinations that would distinguish "existence" from "identity" — and asking what one is thinking when one has thought this. The distinction between *is*-as-existence and *is*-as-identity already presupposes a logic richer than the beginning: it presupposes that there are objects and that there are predicates, with the structure of attribution between them. None of this is available at the beginning. At the beginning, *is* is not yet differentiated into anything. To complain that Hegel runs together two senses of *is* is to insist that the beginning have a structure (two distinguishable senses of *is*) that is exactly what the beginning, taken purely, does not yet have.

So the result stands: pure Being and pure Nothing are not two thoughts that happen to share a property (emptiness); they are *the same thought*, considered under two opposed *meanings*. Their **content** is identical (pure indeterminacy); their **meaning** is opposite (utter affirmation versus utter negation). And this divergence between content and meaning — possible only where mediation has been withdrawn — is the first dialectical insight: a determination that has no internal mediation cannot keep its meaning stable against its opposite. The opposed meaning is generated by the determination's own emptiness.

This is also why one cannot dismiss the move as "merely verbal." A verbal trick would be one in which two distinct contents are made to look the same by some accident of language. Here the contents *are* the same — there is no accident — and the meanings, opposed though they are, are unable to anchor themselves in any difference. That is the substantive philosophical point.

## IV. Becoming as the Truth of Their Identity

What follows from Being's being Nothing and Nothing's being Being?

It does not follow that one is right and the other wrong; both are equally indeterminate, equally pure. Nor does it follow that we should pick a winner; that would be external arbitration. What follows is that the thought-determination we are dealing with is not actually either Being or Nothing taken in isolation — for neither of them, taken in isolation, can sustain itself — but their reciprocal passage, the very movement in which each passes over into the other.

This movement is itself a determination, and Hegel names it: **Becoming** (*Werden*).

Becoming is not a *thing* that joins Being to Nothing — as if Being and Nothing were two pre-existing items between which a relation called Becoming were established. Such an external picture would already have presupposed the determinations as separable, which is precisely what the move shows they are not. Becoming is, rather, the immanent structure that came to light when Being and Nothing were each pursued purely: each, pursued, is the other; the pursuit *of either* is the pursuit *of the other through it*, and *this pursuit-itself* is what Becoming names.

Hegel distinguishes within Becoming two moments:

- **Coming-to-be** (*Entstehen*) — the passage of Nothing into Being. In this moment, Becoming is read from the side of Nothing: what is at issue is how Nothing is, in itself, the passage into Being.
- **Ceasing-to-be** (*Vergehen*) — the passage of Being into Nothing. Here Becoming is read from the side of Being: what is at issue is how Being is, in itself, the passage into Nothing.

These are not two separate movements that happen in sequence (first one comes-to-be, then it ceases-to-be); they are the **same** movement, the very restlessness of pure Being and pure Nothing, read in two different directions. To attempt to think them apart is to find that each contains the other: coming-to-be already contains ceasing-to-be (the Nothing-side of the passage), and ceasing-to-be already contains coming-to-be (the Being-side of the passage). They are inseparable as moments.

Becoming, then, is the first determination of the *Logic* that has **internal articulation**. Pure Being had no inside; pure Nothing had no inside; Becoming has an inside, structured by its two moments. It is also the first place where one can specify, in the *Logic*'s own resources, what *Aufhebung* — sublation — comes to. In Becoming:

- Being and Nothing are **preserved**: they are still present, as the two moments coming-to-be and ceasing-to-be.
- They are **negated** in their false independence: neither survives Becoming as a stable separate determination; each is now a moment of a more comprehensive thought.
- They are **lifted** into a richer determination: Becoming is more concrete than either, in the precise Hegelian sense that it contains determinations (the two moments) that Being and Nothing did not have.

This is the first concrete instance of *Aufhebung*, and the reader who has watched it carefully will recognize that the term is doing real work. It is not a vague gesture toward "synthesis" or "compromise" — Becoming is not a compromise between Being and Nothing, nor an average, nor an oscillation between them. It is what comes to light when each of them, taken purely, is held to itself: the immanent movement that was already in the determination from the start.

A common worry at this point is that Becoming, having been arrived at by its own argument, is now itself just *another* fixed term — that we are simply piling up vocabulary, with no principled stopping-point. The worry would be apt if Becoming were treated as a final determination. It is not. The next move — to which we now turn — is precisely the demonstration that Becoming, taken with the same strictness as Being and Nothing were taken, cannot rest in itself either, and that it has its own sublation, its own truth in a further determination. Every category in the *Logic* will undergo the same fate. Nothing rests, and that very restlessness is what the *Logic* makes systematic.

## V. The Vanishing of the Vanishing

Becoming has two moments — coming-to-be and ceasing-to-be — that are inseparable and reciprocally constitutive. Hold this as strictly as one held the earlier determinations.

When the two moments are taken with full equipoise, they exhaust each other. Coming-to-be is the passage of Nothing into Being, but this passage is at the same time, in Becoming, the ceasing-to-be of Nothing — which is the same passage. Ceasing-to-be is the passage of Being into Nothing, but this passage is at the same time the coming-to-be of Nothing — which, again, is the same passage. The two moments are not two different events; they are the same restless self-cancelling movement, read in two directions. And precisely because they cancel each other — coming-to-be cancels ceasing-to-be by producing what ceasing-to-be cancels; ceasing-to-be cancels coming-to-be by cancelling what coming-to-be produces — the movement consumes itself.

Hegel formulates this with a striking phrase: **the vanishing of the vanishing** (*das Verschwinden des Verschwindens*). The first vanishing was the vanishing of Being into Nothing and of Nothing into Being. The second vanishing is the cancellation of *that* vanishing — the cessation of the oscillation itself.

What remains when the oscillation has come to rest? *Not* simply Being, for the move that arrives here has gone through Becoming, and what arrives is no longer pure Being but Being-as-the-result-of-a-passage. *Not* simply Nothing, for the same reason. *Not* the continuing oscillation, for the oscillation has cancelled itself. What remains is the *settled* outcome: a determinate immediate, a being-this-rather-than-that, a being with a quality.

This is **Determinate Being** — *Dasein*.

It is worth pausing here at the most common contemporary misreading, which is that Hegel is *willing* the result — that he wants the oscillation to settle, and so it conveniently does. The misreading is understandable: every reader feels, at this point, that something is being *produced* rather than merely *found*. But what is being produced is not a new content imported from outside; it is the structure that the cancellation itself leaves behind. To say "the oscillation cancels itself" is already to say "what remains is not the oscillation but its rested outcome." That outcome is *Dasein*. The Hegelian move is not an act of will; it is the registration of what the self-cancellation cannot itself cancel — namely, that there is something here, no longer purely Being and no longer purely Nothing, but a settled being-this.

Hegel's own gloss is that *Aufhebung* in Becoming has three faces: it cancels the pure separability of Being and Nothing (they are no longer two independent absolutes); it preserves them (as the moments coming-to-be and ceasing-to-be); and it lifts them into a determination richer than either (which contains both as moments). When Becoming itself undergoes a further sublation — when its restlessness cancels itself in turn — the structure recurs at one level higher: Becoming's two moments are cancelled in their distinct independence, preserved as the internal articulation of *Dasein*, and lifted into the more concrete category of being-with-a-determination.

## VI. Determinate Being, and Why It Is on Its Own Field

We have arrived at *Dasein*. The question Section II compressed — and which Section III owes a full answer — is now to be answered: why is the abstract *Dasein* genuinely **on its own field**? Why does it not simply collapse back into pure Being, of which it might seem to be a verbal variant?

The answer is that *Dasein* differs from pure Being in three respects, each of which is decisive.

**First, *Dasein* is immediate, but its immediacy is mediated.** Pure Being's immediacy was the immediacy of having no internal structure at all. *Dasein*'s immediacy is the immediacy of *the result*: it is what stands when the movement of Becoming has settled. So although we encounter *Dasein* as a "this" — a settled determinate — the inner structure of that "this" carries the work of its arrival. *Dasein* is *resulted* immediacy. Pure Being could not be resulted, because nothing preceded it; *Dasein* is at every point the residue of a movement.

**Second, *Dasein* contains its negation as a moment of itself.** In pure Being, negation appeared (as Nothing) only by way of the immanent self-collapse of Being; the negation was external to Being in the sense that Being, as a determination, did not yet have a "this rather than that" structure that would carry negation internally. In *Dasein*, by contrast, the very meaning of "being-this" is the negation of "being-other." To be a determinate being is to be this and not that. The negation has come *inside*. It is no longer the abstract opposition Being-vs-Nothing; it is the determinacy *being-this-not-that* — and that is a richer thought.

We have not yet developed the categories *Quality*, *Reality*, *Negation* in their full *Logic*-internal sense, and the next installment will do so. But it can already be said: the negation that pure Being could not contain — and that drove it into pure Nothing — is, in *Dasein*, retained as an internal moment, *as the determinacy of this rather than that*. The instability of pure Being has not vanished; it has been internalized as the determinacy of *Dasein*.

**Third, *Dasein* has an internal field of articulation.** This is what is meant by saying *Dasein* "plays on its own field." Pure Being had no field at all: every determination was outside it, since it admitted of no determinations. *Dasein*, by contrast, immediately admits of an analysis: it has a *quality* (the determinacy by which it is this and not that), it has a *reality* (the affirmative aspect of its determinacy), it has a *negation* (the negative aspect of the same determinacy), and these are not external to it but its own internal moments. The next installment will develop these. The point now is that they are *available* — there is something to develop — whereas at pure Being there was nothing to develop, only the move into Nothing.

This is also what makes *Dasein* the **first concrete category** in the precise Hegelian sense. "Concrete," as Section II §03 already noted, means in Hegel not "sensorily present" but "rich with internal determinations." Pure Being is the most *abstract* of all categories, because it has the fewest determinations (none). *Dasein* is more concrete than either Being or Nothing, because it contains them as sublated moments and adds a determinacy on top. Each subsequent category in the *Logic* will be more concrete than its predecessors in this sense: not closer to sense-experience but richer in the determinations it contains within itself.

A natural worry is that one might be tempted to think *Dasein* "is something" only by furnishing it surreptitiously with empirical content — by thinking, say, of a stone or a sound as the *Dasein* under consideration. This must be resisted. The *Dasein* of the *Logic* is not yet a stone or a sound; it is the *category* under which stones and sounds (and indeed everything determinately existing) fall, but at the level of the *Logic* this category has no empirical filling and needs none. What it has is the structure we have just identified: resulted immediacy, internalized negation, and an internal field of articulation. That is enough, at this level, to constitute a determination — and it is exactly what pure Being lacked.

## VII. The First Dialectical Step, Now Watched From Inside

Pull back. What we have just done — Being → Nothing → Becoming → *Dasein* — is the very first instance of the dialectical step. Section II §02 gave its schema. Section III has now given the schema its full enactment.

Three things are visible from this enactment that the schema alone did not show.

The first is the **compulsion** of each step. At the schematic level, "the determination negates itself" is a phrase. Inside the move, it is a discovery: when one really tries to think pure Being purely, one finds oneself thinking pure Nothing. The negation is not added by the philosopher; it emerges when one attends to the determination. This is what "immanent" means in Hegel: the negation is brought up by the determination itself when held to its own concept, not imposed from outside.

The second is the **conservation** characteristic of *Aufhebung*. At the schematic level, sublation can sound like a magic word that resolves an opposition. Inside the move, it is a structure: Becoming positively contains Being and Nothing as its moments; *Dasein* positively contains Becoming's two moments as its determinacy. The richer category is richer precisely because it carries forward what the poorer categories contained; nothing has been thrown away, only relativized. This is why Hegel can write — and Section II §05 already quoted — that "the truth is the whole": every later determination preserves every earlier one, internalized as a moment.

The third is the **conditionality of the result on the strictness of the attempt**. The move from Being to Nothing — and from there to Becoming, and from Becoming to *Dasein* — only works if one holds each determination strictly to itself. The reader who, at the first step, hesitates and substitutes "existence" or "highest being" or "totality of beings" for *pure* Being will not feel the collapse into Nothing, and will then find the whole subsequent movement willed and arbitrary. Conversely, the reader who really does hold pure Being to itself will feel the collapse not as a surprise but as a necessity. This conditionality is not a defect; it is what the *Logic* asks of its reader. The categories are not free-standing items that one inspects from outside; they are determinations that yield their truth only when thought strictly. To follow the *Logic* is to discipline one's thinking until each category yields what it has to yield.

This is also what distinguishes Section III's mode of presentation from Section II's. Section II could rest on the schema because its task was to make the whole circle visible at once. Section III must instead exhibit the necessity of each step, because its task is to make the experience of the method available to a reader who would otherwise feel the schema as a series of asserted moves. The same dialectical structure that organized the bird's-eye picture is at work here, but at a finer resolution; and at every later step in Section III, the same will be true.

## VIII. What Comes Next

We have arrived at *Dasein* as the first concrete category. *Dasein* is, but it is also already differentiated into an internal field — Quality, Reality, Negation — which the next installment must develop. The same kind of close reading will continue: each internal determination of *Dasein* will be shown to be both indispensable and unable, taken in isolation, to stand. From their tensions will emerge the further categories — *Something* and *Other*, being-in-itself and being-for-other, finitude — and from finitude in turn the move to infinity, which closes the first major sub-circle of the Doctrine of Being.

The reader who has followed this installment carefully has already, in microcosm, learned the discipline that Section III is meant to cultivate: hold the determination strictly; watch it negate itself; identify what its self-negation cannot cancel; let that residue be the next determination. From now on, the same exercise will be repeated at every level. The categories grow in concreteness; the discipline does not change.

## Coda

A reader who has followed Sections I and II to this point may notice a peculiar phenomenon: that the act of working through this installment — slowly, with attention, refusing to let oneself off the hook at the difficult places — is *itself* an instance of what the *Logic* describes. One has been working through the determination of *Dasein* by holding determinate moments strictly to themselves, watching them collapse, and accepting only what remains after the collapse. That is the structure of the method exhibited not in its content but in its enactment. The reader who is conscious of this will already have begun to occupy the standpoint from which the whole circle of the *Logic* becomes available. Section III will help that occupation become habitual.
