# Pure Quantity: Continuity and Discreteness

*The sixth installment of Section III — and the first in the sphere of Quantity. A close reading of **pure quantity** as the sublated being-for-self that the previous installment produced: the determinacy that has become **indifferent** to the being it determines; its two inseparable moments, **continuity** and **discreteness**, and how each is the legacy of attraction and repulsion respectively; why holding the two apart generates the ancient and modern puzzles of the continuum; **Kant's Second Antinomy** (the infinite divisibility of matter versus its ultimate simple parts) dissolved inline as a confusion native to the very concept of quantity; and the **limitation** by which pure quantity gives itself a limit and becomes the **Quantum**, which the next installment will develop.*

---

## I. Where We Are: from the Indifferent Unit to Quantity

The previous installment closed the sphere of Quality. It did so not by reaching a final quality but by watching a quality — the exclusive One — drive its own determinacy into indifference. Repulsion posited many Ones; the many Ones turned out qualitatively indistinguishable; Attraction drew them back toward unity; and the two, shown to be inseparable moments of one self-relating negativity, left behind a manyness of perfectly alike units, externally related, each indifferent to its own place among the rest. That indifference of determinacy to being is **Quantity** (*Quantität*), and the bare repeatable unit that the dialectic left in its wake is the **unit of counting**.

This installment takes up Quantity at its purest — before it has given itself any determinate limit, before it is *this much* rather than *that much*. We will find that pure quantity is not the simple, featureless thing it first appears, but carries within it two moments whose interplay reaches from Zeno's paradoxes to the foundations of the calculus to the quantization of energy. And we will find that the very structure of pure quantity already contains, as moments of one category, the two theses that Kant could only set against each other as an irresolvable antinomy.

## II. What Quantity Is — Sublated Being-for-self

Begin by fixing what kind of determination quantity is, for the whole sphere turns on it. In Quality, the determinacy *was* the being: a thing was *this* by its quality, and to alter the quality was to make the thing cease to be and become another. The qualitative limit was the thing's very edge of being. Quantity is the determination in which this identity of determinacy and being has come apart. A magnitude can grow or shrink, can be more or fewer, *without the thing ceasing to be what it is*. Five is five whether it counts apples, atoms, or angels; the heap remains a heap as you add or remove grains, through a long indifferent range. Determinacy has become **indifferent** (*gleichgültig*) to its being — present, but no longer constitutive.

This is why Hegel calls quantity **sublated being-for-self**. The being-for-self of the One was the sharpest self-assertion of qualitative determinacy: *this* one, excluding all else. Quantity is what that self-assertion becomes once it has worn itself smooth — the One's exclusiveness dissolved into the indifferent repeatability of the unit. Everything the One was is still present in quantity, but *sublated*: the self-relation survives as the self-equality of magnitude, the exclusion survives as the discreteness of the units, the collapse-into-unity survives as the continuity of the whole. Quantity does not arrive from outside the Quality sphere; it is that sphere's own result, carrying its predecessors forward in thinned, indifferent form.

It follows that quantity is, at this stage, a strange kind of determinacy — a determinacy that does not yet determine anything in particular. Pure quantity is just *magnitude as such*: the open dimension of more-and-less, not yet cut at any point into a definite amount. To understand it we must look at the two moments it inherited from the dialectic that produced it.

## III. Continuity and Discreteness — the Two Moments

Pure quantity has two moments, and they are precisely the sublated forms of attraction and repulsion.

The first is **continuity** (*Kontinuität*). The many Ones, recall, proved to be all the same One; their distinction was external and, in truth, null; Attraction was the movement in which their manyness collapsed into self-sameness. That self-sameness, taken as a moment of magnitude, is continuity: the unbroken self-equality of quantity, the flowing-together in which any part is the same as any other and passes into it without a break. Continuity is the legacy of attraction — the moment of *oneness*, of uninterrupted self-identity, in magnitude. The line, the interval, the duration, the smooth gradient: these are quantity with its continuous moment to the fore.

The second is **discreteness** (*Diskretion*). The same Ones, recall, were *many* — Repulsion posited them as a plurality of units, each a self-excluding one. That plurality, taken as a moment of magnitude, is discreteness: the manyness of distinct units of which the quantity is composed, the articulation of magnitude into countable ones. Discreteness is the legacy of repulsion — the moment of *manyness*, of distinct units, in magnitude. The count, the number, the heap of grains, the row of marks: these are quantity with its discrete moment to the fore.

Now the decisive point, and it is the point on which all the puzzles turn: **continuity and discreteness are not two kinds of quantity but two moments of every quantity.** Hegel is emphatic here, and the emphasis is easy to lose. There is a long habit — in mathematics, in physics, in ordinary thought — of treating "continuous magnitude" and "discrete magnitude" as two distinct species: the line versus the number, the analog versus the digital, space versus the count. That habit is not wrong as a rough classification of which moment *predominates*; but it is wrong if it is taken to mean that some magnitudes are *purely* continuous and others *purely* discrete. There is no such thing. Every quantity is both. (A qualification, to match Hegel's order exactly: at the level of immediacy the two moments are not quite on a par. Pure quantity in its first immediacy is *continuous* — the unbroken self-equality is the face it shows first — and discreteness is the moment that *emerges* as quantity posits the one within itself. This is why, in §VI, it is the discrete moment that drives quantity to its self-limitation. The symmetric exposition above is the truth of the matter; but continuity is its immediate face, and discreteness the moment that does the work of moving it on.)

## IV. Why the Two Moments Are Inseparable

Watch the inseparability directly, for it is the heart of the matter.

A purely **continuous** magnitude — magnitude with no discreteness whatever — would be an unbroken self-equality with nothing in it to distinguish one part from another. But a magnitude is a *magnitude*: it is *more or less*, it can be greater or smaller, and that already requires that it be articulable into parts, that there be units against which "more" and "less" are measured. A continuity that could not in principle be divided, counted, marked off, would not be a *magnitude* at all; it would be the indistinct *is* of pure Being all over again. So continuity, to be the continuity *of a magnitude*, must carry discreteness within it: the continuous line *is* divisible at any point, the smooth duration *is* numerable into instants. The discreteness is not added from outside; it is implicit in the continuity as its own other moment.

A purely **discrete** magnitude — a manyness of units with no continuity whatever — would be a heap of absolutely separate ones with nothing holding them together as *one* magnitude. But a count is a count *of something*: the units are units of *the same kind*, they sum into *one* amount, they are members of *one* magnitude. What makes the seven marks *seven* — one quantity, not seven unrelated singularities — is that they are the same unit repeated, flowing together into a single sum. That flowing-together, that self-equality of the units, is continuity. So discreteness, to be the discreteness *of a magnitude*, must carry continuity within it: the number *is* a unity, the count *is* one amount. The continuity is not added from outside; it is implicit in the discreteness as its own other moment.

Each moment, pressed, produces the other. This is the same logical signature we have watched since finitude-and-infinity and again in repulsion-and-attraction: two determinations the understanding holds rigidly apart turn out, read for their own movement, to be moments of one whole, each generating the other. Continuity and discreteness are that whole — *quantity* — seen from two sides.

The puzzles of the continuum are, every one of them, the result of forgetting this. Zeno's runner, who must cross infinitely many half-distances and so (it seems) can never arrive, arises from taking the continuous interval as *nothing but* an infinite discreteness of points and then being unable to sum them. The long labor of the foundations of analysis — Cauchy and Weierstrass taming the limit, Dedekind and Cantor constructing the continuum out of the discrete, the constructivist's refusal of the completed infinite — is, read dialectically, the working-through of exactly this tension between the continuous and the discrete moment of magnitude, the attempt to do justice to both where the understanding can hold only one at a time. And the quantization of energy — the discovery that a quantity the nineteenth century took as smoothly continuous (radiant energy) comes in discrete packets — is the physicist's encounter, in nature, with the truth that no magnitude is purely continuous: the discrete moment, suppressed in the classical picture, asserts itself. We are not, of course, deducing these results; we are locating the *logical form* of which they are so many instantiations.

## V. Kant's Second Antinomy, Answered Inline

The cleanest historical demonstration that continuity and discreteness are moments of one concept — and not two exclusive truths about a thing — is the dissolution of an antinomy that Kant could resolve only by retreating to transcendental idealism — that is, only by declaring both sides false of an unknowable beyond, never within rational cosmology itself.

Kant's **Second Antinomy** sets two theses against each other. The *thesis*: every composite substance consists of simple parts, and nothing exists but the simple and the composite of the simple — the world is, at bottom, atoms. The *antithesis*: no composite thing consists of simple parts, and nothing simple exists anywhere — matter is infinitely divisible, continuous through and through. Kant marshals a proof for each, finds the proofs equally cogent, and concludes that reason falls into unavoidable contradiction when it applies the categories beyond possible experience; the resolution, for him, is transcendental idealism — both theses are false of things-in-themselves, because the world-whole is not a thing-in-itself at all.

Hegel's response is one of the set pieces of the *Logic*, and it is devastating in its economy. The antinomy, he says, is *not* a special affliction that befalls reason at the outer limits of cosmology. It is nothing other than the two moments of the concept of quantity, *torn apart and each asserted as the whole truth*. The thesis is discreteness absolutized: magnitude is *only* its discrete moment, hence ultimate simple units, atoms. The antithesis is continuity absolutized: magnitude is *only* its continuous moment, hence infinite divisibility with no simple anywhere. Each "proof" is cogent because each correctly draws out one genuine moment of quantity; each conclusion is false because it takes its one moment for the whole. The contradiction is real, but it is not a scandal of reason hitting a wall; it is the ordinary, productive contradiction of a concept that *has* two moments, mistaken for a war between two things. Dissolve the "either/or," see continuity and discreteness as the two inseparable sides of every magnitude, and the antinomy simply dissolves with it — not by declaring both theses false of an unknowable beyond, but by recognizing both as *one-sided*, each true as a moment and false as a totality.

This is worth dwelling on, because it shows the difference between the critical and the speculative standpoint at its sharpest. Kant finds the contradiction and *stops* — treats it as a boundary-marker warning reason off. Hegel finds the same contradiction and *reads* it — treats it as the inner articulation of the concept, the very life of quantity. The antinomy that, for Kant, proves the limits of knowledge proves, for Hegel, only that knowledge had been holding apart what belongs together. There is no thing-in-itself here at all; there is a concept with two moments and a habit of mind that can grasp only one moment at a time.

## VI. The Limitation of Quantity — the Quantum

Pure quantity, as we have had it so far, is *unlimited* in a specific sense: it is magnitude as such, the open dimension of more-and-less, not yet cut at any definite point. Continuity and discreteness are its moments, but neither yet fixes it at a *this much*. The smooth line has no particular length; the open count has no particular number. Pure quantity is determinacy that has not yet determined itself to any one magnitude.

But quantity cannot remain merely open, and the reason lies in its discrete moment. Discreteness is the moment of the unit, of articulation, of the *one* and the *many ones*. Where there are units, there is the possibility — indeed the necessity — of *this* collection of them rather than that: of a limit drawn within the magnitude, marking off *so many*, *this far and no further*. Quantity, having discreteness as one of its moments, gives itself a **limit**; and quantity with a limit, magnitude determined as *this much*, is the **Quantum** (*Quantum*).

The quantitative limit is a peculiar thing, and its peculiarity is the engine of the next installment. The *qualitative* limit (the Limit of §12, the Restriction of §13) was the thing's very being: cross it and the thing ceases to be what it is. The *quantitative* limit is the opposite in respect of indifference: the magnitude can be otherwise — larger, smaller — and still be magnitude, still be *this* thing; the limit does not touch the being. And yet it is a limit, a genuine determinacy, a *this much* and not another. So the quantum is determinacy and indifference at once: it is *this* definite amount, and it is supremely indifferent to being *this* amount rather than a neighboring one. That double character — a fixed limit that is intrinsically alterable, a determinacy that points beyond itself to *more* and *less* — is what will, in the next installment, generate number, the distinction of extensive and intensive magnitude, and the specifically *quantitative* form of the spurious infinite.

## VII. Two Misreadings, Answered Directly

Two standing misreadings are worth meeting before we proceed.

The first is the **arithmetical misreading**: that "quantity" just means number, so this whole analysis is a roundabout way of talking about counting. But number has not yet appeared. Number is *determinate* quantity, quantum articulated into amount and unit — and quantum itself is only emerging now, at the end of this installment, out of pure quantity's self-limitation. Pure quantity is prior to number as the open dimension of magnitude is prior to any definite count within it. To read number back into pure quantity is to skip exactly the step — the limitation of quantity — that this installment has been preparing. (We will meet the same temptation, and refuse it, when we come to the quantitative infinite: the endless "ever larger" of the number series is not pure quantity but a feature of the *quantum* and its limit.)

The second is the **physical-continuum misreading**: that continuity means *physical space* (or time) and discreteness means *material atoms*, so Hegel is making claims about the constitution of nature. The misreading is natural, because space and matter are the most familiar instances; but it inverts the order. Continuity and discreteness are moments of *magnitude as such* — of any quantity whatever, including the quantity of a sum of money, a probability, a musical interval, a degree of brightness. Space exhibits continuity and matter exhibits discreteness because they are magnitudes, not the other way around. The *Logic* is not legislating about space and atoms; it is exhibiting the logical structure that space, matter, and every other magnitude must share *as* magnitudes. (This is exactly the lesson of §V: the antinomy of matter's divisibility is not about matter but about quantity.)

## VIII. What the Method Did Here — Observations

Two observations, each holding at later steps as well.

The first: **the sphere of Quantity opens by re-collecting the result of Quality, not by starting afresh.** It would be easy to feel the move into Quantity as a fresh beginning — a new subject, magnitude, with its own vocabulary, displacing the old talk of Something and Other and Limit. The truth is the opposite. Continuity *is* attraction sublated; discreteness *is* repulsion sublated; the indifferent unit *is* the exclusive One worn smooth. Nothing has been left behind; everything has been carried forward in the thinned, indifferent register that the previous installment's discussion of *Aufhebung*'s weight identified as proper to the sphere of Being. The reader who keeps the genealogy in view will never mistake quantity for a primitive — and that is the single most important guard against the scientism that takes the quantitative as bedrock. Quantity *has* a pedigree; it is a result; its indifference to quality is itself a qualitative achievement.

The second: **a contradiction the understanding treats as a barrier is, read speculatively, the inner articulation of a concept.** The Kantian antinomy is the exemplary case, but the lesson is general and it is the very pulse of the *Logic*'s method. Wherever thought finds itself driven to two opposed conclusions each of which seems compelling, the speculative response is not to choose, nor to declare the matter beyond knowledge, but to ask whether the two conclusions are not the two moments of a single concept, each cogent because it draws out a real moment, each false because it absolutizes its moment into the whole. This is the difference between a contradiction that *stops* thought and a contradiction that *moves* it. Almost the whole advance of the *Logic* consists in converting the first kind into the second — and the dissolution of the Second Antinomy is the clearest small model of the conversion that the sphere of Being affords.

## IX. What Comes Next

The next installment develops the **Quantum**:

- **Number** (*Zahl*) — the quantum fully determined as the unity of **amount** (*Anzahl*) and **unit** (*Einheit*); why number is the complete posited form of the quantitative limit, and why arithmetic's operations (counting, the species of calculation) are the working-out of the relation of amount and unit.
- **Extensive and intensive magnitude** — the quantum as a plurality of units (extensive) and the same quantum gathered into a simple *degree* (intensive); why the two are not two kinds of quantity but the same quantum in two forms, and what becomes of the old picture of "intensive" qualities.
- **The quantitative infinite** — the quantum's intrinsic self-transcendence into *more* and *less*, the endless number series, and why this is a *spurious* infinite of exactly the form diagnosed in §13 — together with the long-deferred reading of the **differential calculus**, where the *qualitative* truth hidden in the quantitative ratio first breaks through (the Section II promissory note, now to be redeemed).

With number, the quantum, and the quantitative infinite, the second moment of Quantity will be complete, and the way will be open to the **Quantitative Ratio** and, through it, to **Measure**.

## Coda

We have entered the sphere that the modern scientific mind takes most for granted, and we have entered it by refusing to take it for granted. Quantity looks, to the understanding, like the floor beneath which one cannot dig: the realm of the simply countable, the measurable, the more-and-less, where things are at last clear and manipulable because they have been stripped of their troublesome qualities. The *Logic*'s first word in this sphere is that the floor is a result — that the indifferent unit is a worn-down One, that continuity and discreteness are sublated attraction and repulsion, that the most familiar antinomy of metaphysics is only the two moments of magnitude mistaken for two things. None of this makes quantity less powerful; the whole reach of mathematics rests precisely on the indifference we have been examining, the fact that five is five regardless of what it counts. But it makes quantity *intelligible* — locates it, gives it a genealogy, and so prepares us to watch it do what every determination in the *Logic* eventually does: prove that its indifference cannot be sustained, that the quantitative, pursued far enough, turns back toward the qualitative it had set aside. The quantum, with its restless limit, is the first step of that return. *What looked like the end of determinacy — pure indifferent magnitude — turns out to be only determinacy in its most self-forgetful form, already stirring toward the recovery of all it had forgotten.*
