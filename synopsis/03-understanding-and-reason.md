# Understanding and Reason — and the Dialectical Reconstruction of Scientific Practice

*The three sides of the logical (Verstand, Dialektik, Spekulation), and what becomes of induction, hypothesis, experiment, and mathematical formalism when science is grasped dialectically.*

---

## I. Two Faculties, or One Activity in Two Moments?

The tradition before Hegel distinguished two cognitive faculties: **Understanding** (*Verstand*) — the faculty of distinct concepts, fixed definitions, sharp boundaries — and **Reason** (*Vernunft*) — the faculty of ideas, of the unconditioned, of totality. Kant assigns to Understanding the legitimate constitution of objects of experience and to Reason a regulative role only; for Kant, Reason cannot constitute its objects, and when it tries (in the antinomies) it falls into contradiction.

Hegel accepts the distinction but transforms its meaning. Understanding and Reason are not two separate faculties; they are **two moments of the one activity of thought**. The Encyclopaedia Logic (§79) gives the canonical formulation: every logical content has three sides —

1. **The side of the Understanding** (*das Verständige*) — abstract, fixed, holding determinations apart in clean opposition.
2. **The dialectical or negatively-rational side** (*das Dialektische*) — the immanent self-cancellation of these fixed determinations; their passing-over into their opposites.
3. **The speculative or positively-rational side** (*das Spekulative*) — the grasp of the unity of opposed determinations as moments of a richer concrete whole.

These are **not three separate operations** performed by three different faculties on three different occasions; they are the three constitutive moments of any genuine act of thinking. Every concept, when thought through, displays all three: it is first held fast as itself, then shows itself to demand its opposite, then is grasped together with its opposite in a higher unity. To stop at the first moment is to give us *abstract* understanding; to stop at the second is *skepticism*; to traverse all three is *speculative cognition*.

This trinity is the architecture of every dialectical step we examined in synopsis 02 — and it is the architecture, equally, of every advance in the empirical sciences, whether or not the practitioners are aware of it.

## II. The Indispensable Achievement of the Understanding

It is essential to insist, against the common caricature of Hegel as an "irrationalist" or enemy of clear thinking, that **the Understanding is indispensable** and that Hegel says so emphatically. Without the Understanding's labor of fixing distinctions, there is no thought at all — only the inarticulate flux of representation. Without distinct concepts, there are no objects; without sharp definitions, no science.

The Understanding's accomplishments are the very substance of civilization:

- It carves the world into kinds and species.
- It draws the line between cause and effect, substance and accident, subject and predicate.
- It separates the variables of an experiment so that one can be held constant while another varies.
- It abstracts the mathematical structure of motion from the qualitative diversity of moving things.
- It is the principle of all law, of all classification, of all measurement, of all formal proof.

Hegel goes further: he says the Understanding's power of holding determinations fixed and apart is **the most astonishing and absolute power** there is — for it is the power by which thought arrests the flow of intuition and lifts a determination into the universality of the concept. Without this arrest, there is nothing to think about and nothing to think with. The whole edifice of mathematical natural science — Galileo, Newton, Lavoisier, Maxwell, Mendel — is the work of the Understanding operating with magnificent rigor on its proper terrain.

The dialectical critique of the Understanding is not a critique of its work, but of its **finality**. The Understanding becomes pernicious only when it forgets that its determinations are *finite* — when it absolutizes them, treats its abstractions as the last word, and refuses the further moments of dialectic and speculation. Then it becomes what Hegel calls *abstract* or *one-sided* understanding, the dogmatic counterpart of the very metaphysics it imagines itself to have left behind.

## III. The Negative-Rational (Dialectical) Moment

The dialectical moment is the discovery that the determinations of the Understanding, taken at their word, **cancel themselves**. This is not skepticism — skepticism stops at the cancellation and concludes that thought cannot reach truth. Dialectic recognizes the cancellation as the **immanent self-criticism of a finite determination** and reads it as a positive result: the indication that this determination is one-sided and points beyond itself.

Examples are everywhere in the history of science.

- **The simple and the composite.** Atomism declares matter to consist of indivisible parts. Pursued, this generates contradiction: an indivisible *part* must still occupy space and so be divisible; or, if truly without parts, it is no longer matter. The dialectical result is that "simple" and "composite" cannot be absolutely opposed — actual matter is always the unity of both, which is what subsequent physics, with its fields and bound states and quantum non-separability, has had to learn the hard way.

- **Particle and wave.** Two perfectly clear concepts of the Understanding, mutually exclusive on their own terms, turn out — when the matter is pressed — to be moments of one and the same physical reality. The history of quantum mechanics is the dialectical odyssey by which physics was forced to think a unity that classical Understanding had pronounced impossible.

- **Continuity and discreteness.** The differential calculus rests on the apparent contradiction of an "infinitely small" quantity that is neither zero nor finite. Hegel devotes long pages of the *Logic* to this very point and notes — with a sharpness that the foundations of analysis would not catch up with for a century — that the contradiction is not a defect to be rationalized away but the **actual structure of the matter**: the unity of continuous and discrete, mediated through the concept of measure.

- **Cause and effect.** The Understanding draws a clean line: the cause precedes and produces, the effect follows and is produced. Pursued, the distinction collapses: every cause is itself the effect of an earlier cause, and the *qua-cause* status is not in the thing but in the relation, which is reciprocal — *causa transit in effectum, et effectus in causam*. The dialectical result is the move from causality to **reciprocity** (*Wechselwirkung*) and beyond it to the concept of **organism** and **system**, where each "part" is at once cause and effect of every other.

In each of these the dialectical moment is **not destructive**. The Understanding's determinations are not annulled — they are exhibited as moments of a more concrete category, in which they live on with their finite validity intact. Atomism is not refuted; it is preserved as one moment of a richer concept of matter. Wave and particle are not denied; they are recognized as inseparable aspects of an entity neither can name alone. Cause and effect are not abolished; they are enclosed in the wider category of reciprocity.

This is what *Aufhebung* — sublation — actually means in scientific practice: a more adequate concept supersedes a less adequate one *by containing it as a moment*. The finite concept is not erased; it is given its place.

## IV. The Speculative or Positive-Rational Moment

The speculative moment is the apprehension of the unity that the dialectical moment has uncovered. To "speculate," in Hegel's strict sense, is to think **the determinate unity of opposed determinations** — to hold together what the Understanding has put asunder, without losing the distinction.

Speculation is therefore not vagueness, not mysticism, not a refusal of distinctions; it is the precise opposite of all these. It demands more, not less, conceptual rigor than the Understanding alone, because it requires that one keep both opposed determinations fully alive in their distinction *and* think them as moments of one whole. The whole is not their average, not their compromise, not their alternation, but their living mediation — the concrete concept of which each is a one-sided expression.

The speculative result of any genuine dialectical step is therefore always a **richer, more concrete category** than either of the opposites it has subsumed. *Becoming* is richer than Being or Nothing; *reciprocity* is richer than mere causality; *organism* is richer than mere mechanism; *life* is richer than merely physical-chemical process — without any of the lower terms being lost in the higher.

It is in this sense, and only in this sense, that Hegel's logic is a **logic of the concrete**: every higher determination is more concrete than any lower one, because it integrates more moments. The popular image of philosophy as a movement from the concrete to the abstract is here exactly inverted — *abstract* means impoverished in determinations, *concrete* means rich in determinations, and the development of thought is a continual concretion, not a continual abstraction.

## V. The Reconstruction of Scientific Procedures

We can now ask what becomes of the standard procedures of science — induction, hypothesis, experiment, mathematical formalism — when scientific practice is understood through this trinity of moments.

### 1. Induction

The classical problem of induction (Hume's problem) is that no finite collection of singular instances can yield a strictly universal law. Empiricism, pursued to its limit, undermines its own claim to universality.

Dialectically, this problem is exactly what it should be: an immanent contradiction in the *standpoint of mere empiricism*. Induction taken as a self-sufficient method is finite and one-sided; it presupposes precisely the universals it pretends to derive (the very identification of two events as "instances of the same kind" already deploys a universal). Genuine inductive science is never the mere accumulation of instances; it is the **moment** in which the universal is sought *in* the singular and the singular *in* the universal — a moment that the Understanding alone cannot complete and that requires the speculative grasp of the universal as the inner essence of the singular.

The fruitful inductive sciences (chemistry from Lavoisier, biology from Darwin, genetics from Mendel) succeed not because they have surmounted Hume but because they have, in practice, treated their data as the **manifestation of an inner essence** that the data exhibit but do not constitute. They are, despite themselves, doing dialectic: passing from appearance to essence, from the phenomenon to the law that governs it.

### 2. Hypothesis

Hypothesis — the conjectural positing of a universal that would explain a body of phenomena — is the moment in which the Understanding ventures beyond the given and reaches for the inner ground. Hypothesis is the Understanding's own **dialectical impulse**: its admission that the data do not speak for themselves, that an unobserved structure must be posited to make the observed intelligible.

The dialectical critique of hypothesis is not (as in some positivisms) that it goes beyond what is observable, but that it can rest at the level of **mere conjecture** — at a universal posited externally and tested only by its consequences. Genuine science demands more: that the hypothesized universal be exhibited as the **necessary** structure of the matter, not as one possibility among many that happens to fit. This is the move from hypothesis to **theory**, and from theory to **law** — and from law, ultimately, to the system of categories within which the law has its place. Each step is a step from a more external to a more immanent determination.

The hypothetico-deductive picture of science is not wrong; it is **abstract**. It captures a real moment of scientific practice (the Understanding's reaching beyond the given) but misses the speculative moment in which the hypothesis ceases to be merely posited and is grasped as the necessary form of its content.

### 3. Experiment

Experiment is the moment of **practical mediation**: the question put to nature in such a form that nature must answer. Bacon's great insight — that nature, "vexed" by experiment, yields what it would never reveal to passive observation — is dialectical to its core. The experimenter does not simply *receive* nature; he *constitutes* the conditions under which a determination can show itself in pure form, isolated from its accidents.

Speculatively, experiment is the recognition that the Understanding's distinctions must be **realized in the matter** before they can be confirmed in thought. To separate cause from concomitant, the experimenter physically separates them. To distinguish electric from magnetic effects, the experimenter physically isolates them. The laboratory is the place where the categories of the Understanding are forced upon nature and nature is forced to respond in their terms.

But experiment is also the place where the dialectical moment first appears in empirical clothing. The historical pattern is invariant: a successful experimental program isolates a determination, gives it sharp definition, generates a body of laws — and then, at its boundaries, runs into anomalies that the very precision of the program brings to light. (Mercury's perihelion. The photoelectric effect. The negative result of Michelson–Morley.) These anomalies are not failures of experiment; they are experiment's dialectical fruit — the points at which the finite determination shows itself to be finite, and the call for a more concrete category becomes audible.

### 4. Mathematical Formalism

Mathematics is, for Hegel, the supreme instrument of the Understanding — the science of pure quantitative determination, in which the Understanding's power of holding determinations fixed and apart attains its most refined form. Hegel's respect for mathematics is profound; his criticism is precise.

Mathematics treats its objects under the form of **external relation**. Quantity is the determination in which the *what* of the thing is indifferent — a number is a number, regardless of whether it counts apples or atoms or angels. This is the source of mathematics' generality and of its limit. Where the matter under investigation is genuinely indifferent to its quality (geometry of space, kinematics of mass-points), mathematical treatment is sovereign and complete. Where the matter has an essential qualitative determination — life, mind, history, the political — mathematical treatment captures only its quantitative side and inevitably leaves out what is most essential. The mistake is not to use mathematics; it is to suppose that the mathematized aspect is the **whole** aspect.

The dialectical critique of mathematical formalism, then, is not anti-mathematical. It is anti-**mathematicist**: against the doctrine that the only real properties are those that admit of measurement. Hegel anticipates here the entire long debate between the natural sciences and the human sciences (*Naturwissenschaften* vs. *Geisteswissenschaften*), and his diagnosis is exactly right: where the matter is essentially qualitative and self-determining, the mathematician's "Procrustean bed" of measure flattens it — not because measurement is wrong, but because measurement is a moment of the matter, not its essence.

Modern physics, characteristically, has dramatized this point in its own development: the discovery that some of the most fundamental physical determinations (spin, charge, identity of indiscernibles, entanglement) are **qualitative** structures that mathematics must contort itself to express, rather than simple quantities that mathematics naturally enumerates.

## VI. The History of Physics as a Dialectical Sequence

A glance at the history of physics confirms that its great transitions follow exactly the pattern of synopsis 02:

- **Mechanism** (Galileo, Newton): matter as inert mass acted on by external forces. The Understanding's clearest determination of the physical. *Generates its own boundary*: the inability to account for chemical combination, in which the parts cease to be mere parts and acquire new qualitative determinations.

- **Chemism** (Lavoisier, Dalton, Mendeleev): matter as bearer of qualitative affinities, of combination and decomposition. *Generates its own boundary*: the inability to account for self-organization and self-maintenance, the phenomena of life.

- **Organism** (biology, from Cuvier and Darwin onward): the living being as the self-maintaining unity of its parts, in which each part is what it is only through its function in the whole. *Generates its own boundary*: the question of consciousness, of the subject that knows.

This is precisely the sequence Hegel reconstructs in the Philosophy of Nature — and the sciences have continued in the same direction since: from organism to ecology to the biosphere, from individual mind to social mind, from mechanism back through field theory to relational and topological accounts of physical reality. At each step a more concrete category supersedes a more abstract one **without abolishing it**: classical mechanics is preserved as a limiting case in relativity and quantum theory; chemistry is preserved as the level at which molecular regularities operate; mechanism remains the substrate of organism. The dialectical pattern is unmistakable; what is missing is usually only the practitioners' awareness that this is what is happening.

## VII. Summary: Three Moments, One Activity

| Moment | Faculty | What it does | What it cannot do alone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Understanding (*Verständig*) | Abstract conceptual fixity | Distinguishes, defines, measures, classifies, infers within fixed terms | Recognize the finitude of its own determinations |
| Dialectical (*Dialektisch*) | Negative reason | Exhibits the immanent self-cancellation of finite determinations | Yield a positive result; risks ending in skepticism |
| Speculative (*Spekulativ*) | Positive reason | Grasps the unity of opposed determinations as moments of a more concrete whole | Operate without the prior labor of the Understanding |

These are not optional moments. Every actual act of cognition — every successful scientific theory, every genuine philosophical insight — passes through all three, whether explicitly or tacitly. Hegel's claim is not that scientists ought to start using a new method; it is that the method by which science actually advances is dialectical, and that bringing this to consciousness is what philosophy contributes to science.

## VIII. The Place Won

We have now reached a point at which the contours of Hegel's contribution to the scientific paradigm can be stated with precision:

1. The forms of thought are not external rules of inference; they are the **immanent articulations of any subject-matter**, and a science's method is the self-movement of its content.
2. The Understanding's fixed determinations are **indispensable** but **finite**; their finitude is not a mark of failure but the spring of further development.
3. The advance of any science consists in passing from less concrete to more concrete categories, **preserving** the lower at each step.
4. There is no privileged method standing over the sciences; there is the one logical movement, exhibited in different matters at different levels of concreteness — physical, chemical, organic, mental, social, historical, philosophical.
5. The justification of any science is the **circle of its development**: the beginning is grounded by the totality that issues from it.
