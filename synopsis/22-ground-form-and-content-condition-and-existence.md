# Ground: Form and Content, Condition, and the Emergence into Existence

*The thirteenth installment of Section III — the third in the **Doctrine of Essence**. §21 ended with contradiction resolving as its self-excluding sides **go to ground** (*zu Grunde gehen*), the deeper unity that holds them as sublated being **Ground** (*Grund*). Here that ground is read through — and with it the tradition's fourth "law of thought," **Leibniz's** principle of **sufficient reason** (*nihil est sine ratione*, nothing is without a ground), now **derived** rather than posited, and thereby limited. Ground's first truth is a **reciprocity**: **ground** and **grounded** each are only through the other, and share **one content**, so that grounding forever threatens to say nothing — the content the same on both sides, only the **form** (positedness) differing. **Absolute ground** articulates this into **form and essence**, **form and matter** (*Form und Materie*) and **form and content** (*Form und Inhalt*): the abiding base and the determining movement upon it, the formless matter that must be formed, and their unity as a **content** that carries its form within it. **Determinate ground** then splits into three: the **formal ground**, where ground and grounded have the *same* content and the "ground" merely renames its effect — the **dormitive virtue** (*virtus dormitiva*) by which opium grounds sleep — the empty explanation of the sciences of the understanding; the **real ground**, where the contents *differ* but their connection is left ungrounded, so grounds proliferate arbitrarily; and the **complete ground**, which grounds the connection itself, and so passes into **condition** (*Bedingung*). The scattered **conditions** accumulate; when the ground and the **complete set of conditions** are present, the whole — the **fact itself** (*die Sache selbst*), the **absolutely unconditioned** — steps forth of itself: **when all the conditions of a fact are present, it enters into Existence** (*Existenz*). The mediation sublates itself, the ground sinks away in the act of grounding, and what stands forth is a **mediated immediacy** — essence's first move out of pure inwardness toward **Appearance**. The categorial-not-empirical guardrail holds: the Logic supplies the *form* of grounding, not the ground of any given fact.*

---

## I. Where We Are: From Contradiction to Ground

The previous installment watched the determinations of reflection complete themselves. Identity, questioned, could not keep difference out; difference sharpened through diversity into opposition; opposition, taken at its word, broke into contradiction; and contradiction, far from halting thought, proved to be the very unrest that moves it — and, in moving, to sink beneath itself. The self-excluding sides, unable to subsist, **went to ground** (*zu Grunde gehen*): they perished, and in perishing disclosed the unity that had sustained them all along. That unity, which holds the opposed determinations together as *sublated* moments, is **Ground** (*Grund*). With it essence has its first form of genuine **mediation** — not the passing-over of Being, which vanished into its result, but a mediation that stays with itself, that *is* the identity of the differences it holds.

Ground is therefore not a fourth determination laid beside identity, difference, and contradiction. It is their **resolved unity** — essence posited as the totality that has its own distinguishing within it. Where identity was self-relation that excluded difference, and contradiction was the self-relation that could not exclude it, ground is the self-relation that *contains* difference as its own posited moment and stands firm in it. Essence, as ground, is the self-identical that **grounds** its own determinations.

Here the *Logic* redeems a further promissory note. §21 derived the three "laws of thought" the old logic had merely collected. There is a fourth principle the tradition set beside them and likewise took for a self-evident axiom: the **principle of sufficient reason** — *nihil est sine ratione*, **nothing is without a ground** — Leibniz's great principle, that for everything that is, there is a reason why it is so and not otherwise. Just as the first three laws turned out to be the frozen deposit of the determinations of reflection, so this fourth turns out to be the frozen deposit of **ground** itself. And deriving it, rather than positing it, at once *limits* it. Leibniz himself already sensed the limit: he insisted on **sufficient** reason precisely to mark off a reason adequate to the whole content from the mere efficient causes the mechanists offered — and a ground that must be *sufficient*, that must measure up to the full content of what it grounds, is a ground straining beyond bare ground toward purpose and Concept. The chapter on Ground is the unfolding of exactly that strain: the discovery that the demand "give the ground" is not satisfied by giving *a* ground, and the tracing of what it takes to satisfy it.

## II. Ground and Grounded — the Reciprocity and the Threat of Tautology

Take the relation at its simplest. A ground is a ground *of* something — of a **grounded**; and the grounded is grounded *through* something — through its **ground**. Neither term has the least determinacy on its own. To be a ground just *is* to be the ground of a grounded; to be grounded just *is* to be posited by a ground. They are strict **correlatives**, each the whole relation seen from one side, each collapsing into nothing if the other is withdrawn.

Now watch what this reciprocity does to their **content**. The ground grounds the grounded — but what is *in* the grounded that is not in the ground? Nothing: whatever the ground grounds, it must itself contain, or it could not be the ground of *that*. And what is in the ground that does not pass into the grounded? Nothing: a ground that held something back would to that extent not be the ground of the whole. So **the content is the same on both sides**. What differs is only the **form**: in the ground the content stands as the *essential*, the reflected-into-self, the "because"; in the grounded the same content stands as *posited*, as the immediate that follows, the "therefore." One content, twice — once as ground, once as consequence.

This is the structural seed of a scandal the chapter will not shrink from. If ground and grounded have the very same content, then **to give the ground threatens to say nothing at all** — to restate the grounded in the grammatical form of a ground, with a "because" bolted to its front. The demand for a reason looks as though it could always be met, and met emptily, by echoing back the fact in the tone of an explanation. Whether that threat is realized or overcome is the whole drama of what follows. It is realized, in its purest form, in the **formal ground** (§IV); it is what the **real** and **complete** grounds struggle against; and it is finally answered only when grounding passes over into the gathering of **conditions** (§V). For now the point is only that the difference of *form* is genuine even where the *content* is one: grounding is a real movement in which essence sets its content out as posited and takes it back as essential. Ground is not yet the empty tautology it threatens to become — but it carries the threat in its constitution, and everything turns on how it is discharged.

## III. Absolute Ground: Form and Essence, Matter, and Content

Before ground becomes *determinate* — a ground of this rather than that — Hegel takes it in its still-indeterminate shape, as **absolute ground**: the bare substrate, essence as the simple foundation that underlies. Left at that, "ground" would be an empty word for "whatever underlies." So it must articulate itself, and Hegel does so through three couplings — **form and essence**, **form and matter**, **form and content** — the last two of which the natural sciences and the older metaphysics have quarreled over for two millennia.

**Form and essence.** The first coupling is already implicit in the "bare substrate" just named. Take ground as the simple, self-identical **essence** that persists — and set over against it the whole restless movement of the determinations (identity, difference, positedness) that reflection performs *on* and *in* it: that movement is **form** (*Form*). Essence is the abiding, form the determining; and the two are not two things but one, for the persisting substrate has no content except what form articulates, and form has nowhere to work except in the essence it determines. This is the same lesson the next two couplings will drive home, stated first at its most abstract; the "bare substrate" of absolute ground *is* this form-and-essence unity, still undeveloped. (The point is flagged rather than given its own long treatment: nothing below turns on the abstract form/essence pair that is not shown more concretely in form/matter and form/content.)

**Form and matter.** Take the substrate first as the sheer *persisting base* over against the *activity* that determines it. The base, held as the passive, indeterminate, merely-there stuff, is **matter** (*Materie*); the determining, restless, articulating side is **form** (*Form*). This is the categorial skeleton of Aristotle's hylomorphism — the marble and the shape, the *hylē* and the *morphē* — and its logical lesson is that the two **cannot stand apart**. Matter, thought as utterly formless, is a nothing, an indeterminate that is not even *this* stuff rather than that; it is already, on inspection, informed. Form, thought as pure determining with nothing to determine, has no purchase, no subsistence; it drives of itself to **materialize**, to be the form *of* something. Each **presupposes** the other and **passes into** it: matter must be formed to be matter at all, form must be realized in a matter to be form at all. (The point is categorial, not a physics of "matter" — the *Logic* does not pronounce on quarks or fields; it exhibits the form/matter relation of which every such doctrine is a determinate use.)

**Form and content.** The truth of that mutual passing is that the opposition of an indifferent base and an external shaping cannot be the last word. What we actually have, once form and matter have shown themselves inseparable, is **formed matter** — matter that has taken form *into* itself, form that has become substantial. This unity is **content** (*Inhalt*). Content is the determinate "what" that is neither the bare stuff nor the bare shape but the two grown together: the content of a book is not its paper (matter) nor its grammar (form) but the formed sense they compose. Content has an important double face, and both sides matter for what follows. On one side it is **indifferent** to the outer play of form — the same content can be set out in different forms (proved or narrated, stated in one notation or another). On the other side it has **form essentially within it** — a content is always already articulated, or it would be no determinate content at all. It is this second face — content as carrying its own form — that gives grounding something to bite on: a **ground grounds by way of a content**, and the three determinate grounds are three ways the identity and difference of content can stand between ground and grounded.

## IV. Determinate Ground: Formal, Real, and Complete — and the Critique of Pseudo-Explanation

Ground now becomes **determinate**: a ground of a *definite* content. Three forms arise, in ascending truth, and the ascent is at the same time a sustained critique of how the understanding explains.

**The formal ground.** Here ground and grounded have the **same content**, and the ground-relation contributes nothing but the *form* of ground — the content, already there as fact, is simply presented over again as its own reason. This is the tautological ground, and the *Logic*'s verdict on it is merciless. The immortal illustration is **Molière's**: the physician, asked why opium induces sleep, answers that it has a **dormitive virtue** (*virtus dormitiva*), a power-to-put-to-sleep — which is to say, opium puts to sleep *because* it has the power to put to sleep. The "ground" is the grounded with a "power-to—" prefixed; nothing whatever has been explained. And this is not a rare comic lapse but a standing temptation of the sciences of the understanding, which readily **multiply forces and faculties** cut to the measure of the phenomena they are invoked to ground: a *faculty of memory* posited to ground remembering, a *vital force* posited to ground life, a "*power of attraction*" posited to ground the very falling from which it was read off. In each the ground is manufactured by naming the effect and inserting "the power of," so that the explanation is guaranteed to fit because it is the explanandum wearing a hat. (The verdict is categorial, not a rejection of any particular physics: the *Logic* does not deny that bodies fall or that opium sedates, nor legislate the content of gravitation or pharmacology. It convicts only the *form* of explanation that posits a force or faculty named after the very phenomenon it is summoned to ground — the guardrail of §17–§21 holding here too.) The demand of §II — that a ground merely repeats its content — is here fully realized, and exposed. This is one of the chapter's genuine gifts to scientific method: a sharp categorial name for **pseudo-explanation**, the ground that cannot fail because it asserts nothing.

**The real ground.** To escape tautology, let ground and grounded have **different contents**: the ground is one determination of a thing, the grounded another, and the first is adduced as the reason for the second. This looks at last like real explanation — and it is a step up, for something is now *connected to* something else rather than to itself. But a new defect opens exactly where the old one closed. The **connection** between the two different contents is now left *ungrounded* — it is simply presupposed that *this* determination is the ground of *that*. Two consequences follow, both of them familiar corruptions of reasoning. First, **arbitrariness of selection**: a concrete thing has *many* determinations, and any of them can be picked out and paraded as "the ground" — so that a punishment may be grounded now in deterrence, now in retribution, now in the state's interest, depending only on which side one seizes. Second, **the proliferation of grounds**: since the connection is not itself secured, **grounds can be adduced for anything and against it** — the practiced advocate, the rhetorician, the ideologue can always find *a* ground for the conclusion already wanted, and another for its opposite. Grounding degenerates into a formalism precisely because the essential link is missing. The real ground grounds a content by a content whose bond to it is not itself grounded — and so grounds nothing securely.

**The complete ground.** The truth of the two is a ground that supplies what each lacked: it must have the *identity* of content the formal ground had (so that the ground genuinely belongs to the grounded) *and* the *difference* of content the real ground had (so that it is not an empty echo) — and, above all, it must **ground the connection itself** rather than presuppose it. The **complete ground** does this by referring the two different contents to each other *through* a further term that is their common immediate basis — the determination that is the ground's ground, present as an existent. With that, the ground-relation has driven past itself: it can no longer be a bare "because," suspended in the air, but points to an **immediate that must be there** for the grounding to hold. That immediate is the **condition**.

## V. Condition — the Accumulation, the Unconditioned, and the Fact Itself

A ground, to ground anything actual, needs something to work *on* and *with* — a presupposed, immediate material. This is the **condition** (*Bedingung*), and it has a double character that is the key to the whole close of the sphere. A condition is (i) an **immediate, self-subsistent existent** — it is simply *there*, found, indifferent, not itself produced by this grounding; and (ii) **for the ground** — it is *material* for the grounding, destined to be taken up, sublated, and made into a moment of the grounded. The stone and the mortar are there in their own right; they are also, for the building, its conditions.

Ground and condition are **reciprocally presupposing**. The ground presupposes its conditions — without the material it grounds nothing; and the conditions presuppose a ground — apart from a grounding they are mere existents, conditions *of nothing*. Each is, relative to the other, an *unconditioned* immediate; each is, through the other, *conditioned*. Neither alone is the whole.

But bring the two sides **together** — take the ground *and* the full range of its conditions as one — and something decisive appears. The scattered conditions, gathered and complete, plus the ground that informs them, constitute a **totality that presupposes nothing outside itself**: the **absolutely unconditioned**, which Hegel calls the **fact itself** (*die Sache selbst*) — the whole content, at once its own ground and its own conditioned existence, lacking nothing further to be. And now the chapter's culminating sentence can be spoken, one of the most quoted in the *Logic* and the exact hinge of the transition: **"When all the conditions of a fact are present, it enters into Existence"** (*Wenn alle Bedingungen einer Sache vorhanden sind, so tritt sie in die Existenz*) — where the *fact* (*Sache*) is this self-grounding totality, not yet the *thing* (*Ding*) of §23, which will name a narrower determination within Existence. The *Sache* that was dispersed — partly "in" the ground as its essential content, partly "in" the conditions as their immediate material — is drawn together and **steps forth**.

## VI. The Emergence into Existence

Watch the last step precisely, for it is the signature move of the whole *Logic* enacted in essence's own key. When the conditions are complete, the grounding **does not remain standing beside its product**. The mediation **sublates itself**: the ground, having gathered its conditions and informed them, *sinks away* in the very act — it **goes to ground** once more, this time not as perishing contradiction but as a mediation that consumes itself in fulfilling itself. And what steps forth is therefore **not** a grounded still visibly hanging on its ground, forever pointing back to a "because," but an **immediate** that *is* — a thing that simply stands there, carrying its whole mediation within it as **sublated**. This immediate, come forth from ground, is **Existence** (*Existenz*).

The word is chosen with care: *ex-sistere*, **to step forth**. Existence is not the abstract, unmediated immediacy of pure Being with which the *Logic* opened (§10) — the emptiest of thoughts, presupposing nothing. It is a **mediated immediacy**: essentiality that has *issued from* its ground and shed the ground in issuing, so that its very immediacy is the *result* of a mediation now internalized. With this, essence makes its **first move out of pure inwardness**. Reflection — the shining of essence *within itself*, which has occupied the sphere since §20 — turns outward: what has come forth from ground must now *show itself*, must have its essentiality *in* its existing, and that is the theme of the sphere ahead, **Appearance** (*Erscheinung*). Essence ceases to be only the negativity behind the scenes and begins to *be there*, in a being that is its own manifestation.

The pattern deserves marking, because it will govern every major seam of the *Logic*. A sphere's final mediation does not add one more link to a chain; it **cancels itself into an immediate** that opens the next sphere at a higher register. So Becoming, the restless mediation of Being and Nothing, sublated itself into the quiet immediacy of *Dasein* (§10); so, at the summit, the mediations of cognition will sublate themselves into the immediacy of the Absolute Idea and its release into Nature. Here it is essence's turn: the mediation of ground and condition cancels itself into the immediacy of Existence — but an immediacy now **saturated with essence**, which is why the being that follows is no longer bare *Dasein* but a **thing that appears**.

## VII. Three Misreadings, Answered Directly

**The first is the rationalist-license misreading**: that the principle of sufficient reason, once granted, licenses the giving of reasons *a priori* — that because "nothing is without a ground," a ground may always be produced from thought alone, and the world read off from principles. The chapter's derivation says the reverse. The **formal** ground was exposed as empty and the **real** ground as arbitrary precisely to show that the demand "give the ground" is *not* met by producing *a* reason; it is met only by the **complete ground** and, beyond it, by the **totality of conditions** from which the thing steps forth. Understood, the principle of ground drives thought *away* from the armchair and *toward* the concrete, conditioned *Sache* — the opposite of a license for deduction from bare principles. That a ground must be *sufficient* is a demand no isolated reason can satisfy.

**The second is the ground-is-just-cause misreading**: that Ground is a rough first sketch of **causality**, and the chapter a clumsy theory of cause and effect. Ground is **not yet** cause, and the difference is instructive. Ground and grounded share **one content** and differ only in **form** (positedness); cause and effect, too, share their content — Hegel is emphatic that there is nothing in the effect that was not in the cause — but they are distinct **actualities**, bound by a real, transitive **efficacy** in which the cause *acts* and passes over into the effect, and causality does not arise until the sphere of **Actuality**, several steps ahead, where the terms have the standing of the actual. To read ground as cause is to skip the whole ascent still to come — Existence, the Thing, Appearance, the essential relations, substance — and to lose the reason causality has to be *earned* rather than assumed. Ground is a **reflective** relation; cause is an **actual** one.

**The third is the explanation-is-always-empty misreading**: the deflationary over-reading of the dormitive-virtue verdict — that Hegel has shown *all* grounding to be a circle, so the scientific search for grounds is vanity. The verdict falls on the **formal** ground *alone*. Its whole function is to be the *deficient* case whose emptiness **drives** the movement onward — to real ground, to complete ground, to the totality of conditions, that is, to **genuine** explanation. The critique of the pseudo-ground is a **standard**, not a nihilism: it tells us what a ground must *not* be (a renamed effect) in order to sharpen what a ground must *be* (the sufficient, connection-grounding totality from which the thing necessarily follows). Far from dissolving explanation, the chapter is a theory of what would count as completing one.

## VIII. What the Method Did Here — Observations

The first observation is that **the fourth "law of thought" has been derived and, in the deriving, limited.** §21 located identity, non-contradiction, and excluded middle in the determinations of reflection; §22 locates the **principle of sufficient reason** in ground — the resolved unity of those determinations. And the derivation is at the same time a critique: the principle is not a self-evident axiom guaranteeing that reasons are always to be had, but the *form* of essence's mediation, whose bare and formal readings the chapter itself convicts of emptiness. The tradition's four commandments of thought are now, all four, exhibited as **stations of a movement** rather than a bedrock of first principles.

The second observation is that **a working criterion for scientific explanation has been extracted.** The formal/real/complete triad is a usable diagnostic. A ground that merely **renames its effect** — a power, faculty, or force posited from the very phenomenon it is called to ground — is a **formal** ground and explains nothing (the dormitive virtue, the vital force, faculty psychology). A ground that adduces a **different content** but leaves the **connection ungrounded** is a **real** ground and remains arbitrary — grounds can then be marshaled for any thesis and its contrary. Only the **complete** ground — the totality of conditions from which the *Sache* necessarily steps forth — genuinely grounds. This is a categorial articulation of the difference between **pseudo-explanation, partial explanation, and complete explanation**, and it is precisely the kind of result this synopsis has been tracking: a logical determination that turns out to legislate for method.

The third observation is that **grounding grounds by disappearing** — the self-sublation of mediation into immediacy, now enacted in essence. Ground does not sit beside its product as a permanent support; when the conditions are complete, the ground **sinks away** and the thing stands forth *immediately*, its mediation internalized as sublated. This is the same rhythm that carried Becoming into *Dasein* and will carry cognition into the Idea — the deepest formal signature of the *Logic* — but here it earns a specific content: it is **why essence can leave its inwardness at all**, why the sphere of hidden reflection tips over into a sphere of things that *appear*. Mediation completing itself as immediacy is not a trick of exposition; it is how a category ceases to point beyond itself and comes, instead, to *be*.

## IX. What Comes Next

With ground sublated into the immediacy that issues from it, essence has stepped out of pure reflection, and the sphere can develop what has come forth:

- **§23 — Existence and the Thing**: **Existence** (*Existenz*) as the immediate sprung from ground, no longer bare *Dasein* but a mediated immediacy; the **thing** (*Ding*) and its **properties** (*Eigenschaften*); the thing-**in-itself** and the collapse of its supposed hidden core; the dispersal of the thing into a play of **matters** and the passage into **Appearance** proper.
- **Beyond**: **Appearance** (*Erscheinung*) — the world of appearance, appearance and **law**, and the **essential relations** (whole and parts, force and its expression, inner and outer) — and then **Actuality** (*Wirklichkeit*) — modality, substance, and at last **causality** and **reciprocity**, where the transitive efficacy that ground could only prefigure finally arrives, essence and appearance are fully united, and the sphere passes over into the **Concept**.

## Coda

The tradition's fourth commandment — *nihil est sine ratione*, nothing is without a ground — looked like a standing license: everything has its reason, so a reason may always be given. We have watched the giving-of-reasons turn on itself. The reason that merely renames its effect — opium's dormitive virtue, the force read off the very falling it is summoned to explain — grounds nothing, being the fact with a "because" pinned to it. The reason picked out from a many-sided thing grounds arbitrarily, and the advocate who can find one can find its opposite. It is only when *all* the conditions are gathered that the thing steps forth of itself — and at that very moment the ground, its work done, **sinks away**, so that what stands before us is no longer a grounded clinging to its ground but a thing that simply *is*. *A ground proves sufficient not when it can be recited but when it has spent itself: the reason we were owed turns out to be the thing's own coming-into-existence — the ground gone zu Grunde, gone under in the act of grounding, precisely so that existence might stand forth and, having stood forth, begin at last to show itself.*
