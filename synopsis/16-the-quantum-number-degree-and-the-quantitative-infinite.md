# The Quantum: Number, Degree, and the Quantitative Infinite

*The seventh installment of Section III — a close reading of the second moment of Quantity. The **quantum** (*Quantum*) as quantity with a limit, and the peculiar **indifference** of that limit; **Number** (*Zahl*) as the quantum's complete posited form, the unity of **amount** (*Anzahl*) and **unit** (*Einheit*), with the species of calculation read as the progressive internalizing of their relation; **extensive** and **intensive magnitude** (*Grad*, degree) as not two kinds of quantity but the same quantum in two forms, and the critique of the old picture of "intensive" qualities; the **quantitative infinite** — the quantum's restless self-transcendence into *more* and *less*, the **spurious** quantitative infinite of the endless series and the asymptote (the form already diagnosed in §13, now in quantitative dress); and the long-deferred reading of the **differential calculus**, where the vanishing magnitude proves to be nothing outside its **ratio**, and the ratio's content proves to be **qualitative** — the Section II promissory note, now redeemed, and the first decisive return of quality within the sphere of quantity.*

---

## I. Where We Are: from Pure Quantity's Self-Limitation to the Quantum

The previous installment left pure quantity in the act of limiting itself. Pure quantity was magnitude as such — the open dimension of more-and-less, continuous and discrete in its two moments, but not yet cut at any definite point into *this much*. Its discrete moment, the moment of the unit and the many ones, would not let it stay open: where there are units there is the possibility, and the necessity, of *this* collection of them rather than that, of a limit drawn within the magnitude. Quantity giving itself such a limit is the **quantum** — magnitude determined as *this much*, *so many*, *this far and no further*.

This installment develops the quantum through its three forms. First the quantum gives itself its complete and proper expression as **Number**. Then the quantum shows that it can be taken in two ways — as **extensive** magnitude (a plurality of units) and as **intensive** magnitude or **degree** (a simple intensity) — which prove to be the same quantum in two forms. Then the quantum's limit, pressed, reveals the same restlessness the finite had: every quantum points beyond itself to a larger and a smaller, and this is the **quantitative infinite**. Following that restlessness to its truth — through the spurious infinite of the endless series and into the differential calculus — we will watch the quantum so thoroughly transcend itself that what remains is no longer a quantum at all, but a *qualitative* relation. That is the moment quality returns within quantity, and it is the hinge on which the whole sphere turns toward Measure.

## II. The Quantum and Its Indifferent Limit

Recall the peculiarity of the quantitative limit, for everything that follows is its unfolding. The *qualitative* limit was the thing's very being: a Something was *this* up to its limit, and to cross the limit was to cease to be what one was. The *quantitative* limit is indifferent to being. A magnitude can be larger or smaller and remain magnitude; the thing whose magnitude it is can take on more or less and remain that thing — the heap is still a heap, the line still a line, the sum of money still a sum of money. And yet the quantitative limit is a *genuine* limit, a real determinacy: the quantum is *this* amount and not another.

So the quantum carries a contradiction in its very constitution, and it is a sharper contradiction than first appears. The quantum *is* its limit — it is nothing but *this much*, wholly determined by the point at which it is cut. And the quantum is at the same time *indifferent* to its limit — it could be otherwise and still be a quantum, still be *this* thing's magnitude. A determinacy that is at once the whole being of the thing-as-magnitude and a matter of indifference to it: that is the quantum. We will see this double character drive the quantum, exactly as the analogous double character drove the finite, beyond every limit it reaches.

## III. Number — the Quantum's Complete Form

The quantum's proper and complete expression is **Number** (*Zahl*). Number is where the two moments of pure quantity — continuity and discreteness — reappear, now within the determinate quantum, as its two constitutive determinations.

The first is the **unit** (*Einheit*). Every number is counted in units of *something*: a unit that is self-same, repeatable, one-and-the-same throughout the count. The unit is the continuous moment within number — the self-equality in virtue of which the many counted ones are ones *of the same kind*, members of one number. The second is the **amount** (*Anzahl*) — *how many*: the determinate plurality of units, the discrete moment within number, the manyness that says *this* number is seven and not five. Number is the unity of the two: a determinate amount of a determinate unit. "Seven" is seven (amount) ones (unit); and the two are inseparable, for an amount is always an amount *of* units, and a unit is a unit only as countable *into* an amount.

This is more than a definition; it is the key to why arithmetic has the structure it does. The operations of arithmetic — Hegel calls them the *species of calculation* (*Rechnungsarten*) — are, read dialectically, the progressive **internalizing of the relation between amount and unit**: they begin with numbering and then fall into three pairs of ascending intimacy.

- **Numbering** is the first and most external: simply positing an amount of units, aggregating ones into a number. Here unit and amount are merely brought together from outside.
- **Addition and subtraction** relate numbers that are still wholly indifferent to one another — any number to any number. The relation is external: 7 and 5 have nothing to do with each other until I choose to add them.
- **Multiplication and division** tighten the relation: now one number is taken *as* a unit and the other *as* the amount of those units (3 × 4 = four threes, or three fours). The two numbers play the *roles* of unit and amount with respect to each other; the relation has become internal to the operation.
- **Raising to a power and extracting a root** complete the movement: in the power a number is multiplied *by itself* — the unit and the amount are the *same number*. In the square, the number is at once that-which-is-counted and how-many-times. Amount and unit, which began as two indifferent determinations brought together from outside, have collapsed into identity. (Extracting the root is the *inverse* operation — the analytic return from the power back to the self-related base it presupposes — and so belongs to the same final pair, the one in which the quantum has folded back upon itself.)

That last point is worth pausing on, because it is the seed of everything in this installment's second half. In the power, the quantum is related to *itself*: it is its own unit and its own amount. A self-relation has appeared within the merely external realm of number. And a self-relation is precisely the form of the *qualitative* — recall that quality was the determinacy that *was* the being, the thing's self-relation. So in raising-to-a-power, the qualitative makes its first faint return *inside* quantity, in the very heart of arithmetic. This is not yet developed; but mark it, for the calculus will be its full unfolding.

## IV. Extensive and Intensive Magnitude — One Quantum, Two Forms

The quantum, as number, presents itself first as **extensive magnitude** (*extensive Größe*): magnitude as an *amount*, a plurality of units spread out — a hundred is a hundred ones, a length is so many inches, a duration so many seconds. The "extensive" name marks that the determinacy lies in the *manyness*, the units laid side by side.

But the same quantum can be taken in another form. The hundredth degree on a scale, the temperature, the pitch, the intensity of a colour or a force, the *degree* of anything — these are magnitudes that are *not* presented as a plurality of units within themselves. The hundredth degree is not "a hundred units of heat" lying side by side inside it; it is a *simple* determinateness, an intensity, a single *grade*. This is **intensive magnitude** or **degree** (*Grad*).

The understanding takes extensive and intensive magnitude for two different *kinds* of quantity — even for two different *realms*, the extensive belonging to the countable and divisible, the intensive to the qualitative, the inward, the unmeasurable-from-within. Hegel's analysis dismantles this, and the dismantling is one of the quietly decisive moves of the sphere.

First: **extensive and intensive magnitude are the same quantum in two forms, and each passes into the other.** The degree, though simple, is a *determinate* degree — *this* intensity and not another — and its determinateness is nothing other than its place in the scale, its relation to the degrees above and below. But a place in a scale is a *number*: the hundredth degree is the hundredth, the amount one hundred has not vanished but only been gathered up into the simple "hundredth." And conversely, every extensive magnitude can be gathered into an intensive one: the hundred units of length *are* the magnitude "one hundred," which can be read off as a single point on a measuring rod. The two are convertible — once a scale is in place — because they are the same quantum, taken once as spread-out amount and once as gathered intensity.

Second — and this is the polemical edge — **the degree's apparent inwardness is an illusion; its determinacy is wholly external to it.** Just because the degree is *simple*, having no plurality of units *within* itself, it has nothing in itself by which to be the determinate degree it is. What makes it the hundredth and not the ninety-ninth lies entirely *outside* it, in the series of other degrees, in the whole scale. The intensive magnitude, which seemed the very type of self-contained inwardness, turns out to be the *most* externally determined of all: it depends for its entire determinacy on a whole series of others to which it is indifferent. The old picture — degree as a qualitative intensity that has escaped the externality of counting, the "latitude of forms," intensity as an inward more-or-less proper to qualities themselves — is exactly inverted. Degree is not quantity become inward; it is quantity at its most external, wearing the mask of inwardness. (This matters for the sciences: it is why an "intensity" — temperature, brightness, utility, felt pain — *can in principle* be set against a scale, a number, an extensive measure, once such a measure has been established; the mere simplicity of degree is no barrier to it. The intensive is not a fortress against quantification; it is quantification in its most concentrated form. What genuinely resists is not intensity but *quality*, and quality returns not here but through the road this installment is about to take.)

## V. The Quantitative Infinite — the Quantum's Self-Transcendence

Now the quantum's indifferent limit (§II) does its work. The quantum is *this much* — but because its limit is indifferent, because it could always be more or less, the quantum points beyond itself, of its own nature, to a larger quantum and a smaller. And the larger and smaller are themselves quanta, each again pointing beyond itself. The quantum, simply by being the kind of determinacy it is, *sends itself past every limit it reaches*. This self-sending-beyond is the **quantitative infinite**.

We have seen this shape before. It is, exactly, the structure of the finite and its spurious infinite from §13, now in quantitative dress. There, the finite's nature was to pass beyond its limit, generating the endless progress — the "and so on," the always-one-more, the beyond that recedes as fast as it is approached. Here the quantum's nature is to pass beyond its limit, generating the endless number series — *n*, *n*+1, *n*+2, … with no last term; the always-larger and always-smaller; the asymptote that the curve approaches forever and never reaches; the infinite series whose terms never come to an end. This is the **spurious quantitative infinite** (*das schlechte quantitative Unendliche*), and Hegel's verdict on it is the same as before: it is no true infinite at all. It is the perpetual *positing* of a beyond and the perpetual *failing* to reach it; it remains forever entangled with the finite quantum it was supposed to surpass, producing at every step another finite quantum. The "infinitely large" and "infinitely small," taken as ever-growing and ever-shrinking quanta, are spurious infinites: they are quanta still, only quanta with the flag of "infinite" planted on a magnitude that never stops being a magnitude.

The mathematicians' own unease testifies to this. The "infinitely large" number that is yet always a definite number under the limit-sign; the "infinitely small" that must be neither a finite magnitude nor zero; the convergent series "equal to" its sum only in the limit, "at infinity," which is to say never as a completed term — all of this is the spurious infinite generating the very puzzles that the foundations of analysis spent two centuries trying to lay to rest. (We are not adjudicating those technical efforts, and we should be careful not to make Hegel a partisan in them: his interest is not in repairing the calculus but in reading the *logical form* that its difficulties expose. He is notably guarded about the foundations and does not simply bless the completed infinite series as an actual infinite.) What he wants is not a better handling of the endless progress but its *transformation* — the recovery, within the quantitative, of a **true** infinite. And that recovery is the differential calculus, read rightly.

## VI. The Calculus: the Vanishing Magnitude and the Qualitative Ratio

Here the Section II promissory note is redeemed. This is the most technically contested stretch of Hegel's *Logic* — the precise relation of his reading to Newton, Leibniz, Euler, Lagrange, and to the later rigorization is disputed in the literature, and we should hold the *interpretation* loosely. But the *logical* point is clear, and it is profound.

Take the differential, the "infinitely small" *dx*. The understanding asks: what magnitude is it? And it cannot answer. *dx* cannot be a *finite* magnitude — no finite magnitude is infinitely small; any finite *dx* would leave the calculation only approximate. And *dx* cannot be simply *zero* — zero would annihilate the calculation, give 0/0, yield nothing. So *dx* is a magnitude that is *vanishing*: not a fixed quantum at all, but a quantum *in the act of disappearing*, on the very threshold between something and nothing. As an independent quantum, *dx* is indeed nothing; the understanding is right that, taken by itself, it has no determinate value.

Hegel's resolution — and this is a *logical* reading of the historical infinitesimal notation, not a rival rule for assigning *dx* a value (least of all the modern stipulation *dx* = 0) — is to deny that it should be taken by itself at all. **The differential has no meaning as an independent quantum; it has meaning only as a moment of a *ratio*.** Outside the quotient *dy/dx*, *dx* is nothing and *dy* is nothing. But the *ratio* *dy/dx* is not nothing — it has a perfectly determinate value.

Here is the decisive inversion, and it deserves to stand alone: **two magnitudes that have each *individually* vanished to nothing nonetheless stand in a *determinate ratio*.**

The quotient is not the indeterminate 0/0 the understanding fears; it is determinate — but it is determinate as a **ratio**, not as a quantity. The terms have vanished *as quanta* precisely so that what they were holding apart — their *relation* — can stand forth pure.

And what is that relation? It is **qualitative**, not quantitative. *dy/dx* is not the quotient of two surviving magnitudes; it is the *rate*, the slope, the *kind* of dependence that *y* has on *x* — the law of the curve *at a point*, the function's qualitative character there. It is one number, yes, but a number that is the expression of a qualitative determinateness: how, in what manner, this variable depends on that. (Throughout, "qualitative" names this determinate *manner* or *law* of dependence — the *how* of the relation — not some non-quantitative empirical feel; the rate is still a number, but a number that says *in what way*, not merely *how much*.) The differential coefficient is a *quantum that has become the sign of a quality*. This is why the operation works: in passing to the differential we have not found a smaller quantum but *left quantum behind*, distilling out of the vanishing magnitudes the qualitative relation they embodied.

This is the **true quantitative infinite**. The quantum has so thoroughly transcended itself — pushed its self-sending-beyond all the way to the vanishing of magnitude as such — that what remains on the far side is no longer a quantum awaiting a still-larger or still-smaller successor (that was the spurious infinite, the endless series), but a *qualitative relation* in which the beyond is *present*, gathered, determinate. The infinite is no longer the unreachable next term; it is *here*, in the ratio, as the curve's actual slope. The endless approximation of the series is sublated: where the series expresses a determinate law, its truth is the finite, qualitative expression — the derivative, the function, the ratio — of which that series was only the spuriously-infinite unfolding. (Note the exact parallel to §13: the true infinite was not the endless line but the circle, the beyond brought home as a moment of a present whole. The true quantitative infinite is the same victory, won now in the element of magnitude.)

Recall §III's seed: in raising-to-a-power the quantum was first related to *itself*, amount and unit coinciding — the first faint return of self-relation, of the qualitative, within number. The calculus is that seed in flower. It is no accident that *on the route Hegel privileges* — the one that derives the calculus from the expansion of powers, broadly Lagrange's algebraic route — the differential lives among the *powers* and the *power series*: the derivative of *xⁿ* is got by an operation on the exponent, and Hegel locates the whole "qualitative" content of the calculus in the relation a magnitude bears to its own powers. The self-relation that first stirred in the square is, in the calculus, fully posited as a qualitative relation that quantity carries within itself.

## VII. Three Misreadings, Answered Directly

The quantum invites three confusions worth meeting head-on.

The first is the **mathematician's foundational misreading**: that Hegel is offering a *rival foundation* for the calculus — a philosophical theory of infinitesimals competing with limits, or with Weierstrass's *ε–δ*, or with Robinson's non-standard analysis. He is not, and to read him so is to lose his point and to expose him to easy refutation. Hegel is not in the business of grounding the calculus' technique; he grants that the technique works and that its rigorous foundation is the mathematician's affair. His claim is *logical*: that the success of the technique, however one founds it, exhibits a determinate logical form — the passage of quantum, through self-transcendence, into qualitative ratio. Whether one reaches *dy/dx* through vanishing magnitudes, through limits, or through non-standard infinitesimals, what one *reaches* is a qualitative determinateness wearing quantitative dress, and that is all Hegel needs. (This is why the contested status of his infinitesimal-talk does not touch the load-bearing thesis.)

The second is the **intensive-as-qualitative misreading**: that degree, being simple and inward, is already the return of quality within quantity — that intensity is where magnitude becomes qualitative. §IV is the answer: degree is not quantity become qualitative but quantity become *most external*, its whole determinacy lodged outside it in the scale. Intensity is a mask, not a threshold. The genuine return of quality comes not through the simplicity of the degree but through the self-relation of the quantum in power and differential — through the ratio, not the grade.

The third is the **bad-infinity-as-achievement misreading**: that the endless number series, the ever-larger and ever-smaller, the completed infinite of set theory, is the mathematical realization of the true infinite. It is the opposite: the endless series is the *spurious* infinite par excellence, the quantitative form of the very "and-so-on" that §13 unmasked. The true infinite in mathematics is not the inexhaustible series but the *finite expression* — the function, the ratio, the closed form — in which the series' endless beyond is brought home as a present, qualitative determinateness. (We say this without legislating about Cantor: the point is logical, about which *form* deserves the name "true infinite," not a verdict on transfinite set theory's mathematical legitimacy.)

## VIII. What the Method Did Here — Observations

Two observations, each carrying past this installment.

The first: **quality returns within quantity by quantity's own movement, not by importation from outside.** This is the structural heart of the whole sphere and the answer to the scientism §15 warned against. Quantity set out as the determinacy indifferent to quality — the realm where the *what* drops away and only the *how-much* remains, the ground the measuring sciences stand on. We have now watched that very indifference undo itself *from within*. The quantum's indifferent limit drove it past every magnitude; the self-sending-beyond, pursued to the vanishing point, distilled out a *ratio*; and the ratio's content turned out to be *qualitative* — a rate, a law, a *kind* of dependence. No quality was smuggled in; the quantitative, followed honestly to its end, *produced* the qualitative as its own truth. The sciences that take the quantitative as bedrock have, in their most powerful instrument — the calculus — been operating across this very threshold all along, computing qualities (rates, fields, gradients, laws) in the guise of computing magnitudes.

The second: **the same logical form recurs across spheres in a new element, and recognizing it is most of the work.** The quantitative infinite is not a new and separate problem; it is the finite-and-its-spurious-infinite of §13 transposed into magnitude, with the very same resolution — the beyond brought home into a present whole (there the circle, here the ratio). To have grasped the form once is to recognize it everywhere, and a great part of the *Logic*'s economy is this recurrence-with-variation. The reader who learned in §13 to distrust the endless progress and to look for the true infinite that holds its beyond as a moment is exactly equipped to see, in *dy/dx*, not a paradox of the infinitely small but the quantitative homecoming of the infinite.

## IX. What Comes Next

With number, degree, and the quantitative infinite, the quantum is complete — and it has already shown, in the calculus, that its truth is a *ratio*. The next installment develops the third and final moment of Quantity, the **Quantitative Ratio** (*das quantitative Verhältnis*), as the quantum's own truth made explicit:

- The **direct ratio** (the simple proportion, with its **exponent**), where each quantum is what it is only in its proportion to the other, and the exponent — itself a quantum — already does a *qualitative* work, being what makes *this* ratio this ratio.
- The **inverse ratio** and the **ratio of powers**, in which the qualitative role of the exponent deepens until the relation, not the related quanta, carries the determinacy.
- The passage by which the quantitative determinacy, now inseparably bound up with a qualitative one, ceases to be merely quantity: the threshold of **Measure** (*Maß*), the unity of quality and quantity — and with it the close of the whole second sub-circle of the Doctrine of Being, and the gateway (through the nodal line of measure-relations) toward Essence.

The example we deferred from §15 — the quantization of energy — finds its home there, in Measure, as a nodal point: a quantum welded to a quality, where crossing a threshold is a qualitative leap.

## Coda

We entered Quantity at the place modern thought takes for its bedrock, and we have now watched the bedrock open. The quantum looked like the simplest thing in the world — *this much*, a definite magnitude, the very atom of the measurable. And we have seen it carry, in its indifferent limit, the same restlessness the finite carried; seen it send itself past every magnitude into the spurious infinite of the endless series; and seen that restlessness, pursued without flinching to the vanishing of magnitude itself, deliver up not a smaller quantum but a *ratio* — and the ratio's content is no longer a how-much but a *how*, a quality. The most quantitative instrument humanity possesses, the calculus, turns out to compute qualities in the costume of magnitudes. This is the sphere's great irony and its deepest lesson: quantity, the determination that defined itself by its indifference to quality, cannot sustain that indifference, and discloses quality again as its own innermost truth — not by abandoning the quantitative but by carrying it through to the end. *The number, followed far enough, stops being merely a number; in the ratio of its own vanishing it speaks, for the first time since we left Quality behind, the language of the qualitative — and what speaks there is Measure, where the two are one.*
