# Being-in-itself, Being-for-other, and the Limit

*The third installment of Section III — a close reading of the inner structure of the Something. Its two sides — **being-in-itself** (*Ansichsein*) and **being-for-other** (*Sein-für-Anderes*) — are extracted from the contradiction §11 left open; their inseparability is shown to require **Determination** (*Bestimmung*) and **Constitution** (*Beschaffenheit*) as the modes in which the two sides are actually present in any Something; and from their reciprocal play emerges the **Limit** (*Grenze*) — the precise point at which the Something both is and is not itself, and which therefore opens the path to **Finitude**.*

---

## I. Where the Previous Installment Left Us

§11 closed with a contradiction. The Something was shown to be constitutively related to an Other — every "being-this" is at once a "not-being-the-other," and the *not-being-the-other* is internal to the Something's own determinacy, not a comparison added from outside. But that produced a difficulty. If the Something is *exhaustively* describable as "that which is not the Other," then there is nothing left to call "Something" apart from its negative relation to its other. The self-relation we needed (a Something is *one*, an *it that has* its quality) collapsed back into a bare placeholder for negation.

The contradiction is real, and §11 named the only possible resolution: the Something must have *both* a side that is what it is *in relation to its Other* and a side that is what it is *independently of, or in distinction from*, that relation. These two sides cannot be denied (the Something would dissolve into either pure relation or pure isolation), and they cannot be conflated (they would lose the very distinction that makes them resolve the contradiction). They must be **distinguished within the Something itself**.

These two sides Hegel names **being-in-itself** (*Ansichsein*) and **being-for-other** (*Sein-für-Anderes*). The task of this installment is to develop them — what they are, what they are not, why their reciprocal play forces the categories of **Determination** and **Constitution**, and how their interaction produces the **Limit**, from which the entire arc to Finitude and Infinity will follow.

## II. Being-in-itself and Being-for-other

Take the Something. It has a Quality (§11): a determinacy in which Reality and Negation are unified. It is correlative to an Other. Distinguish, now, two aspects under which this very same Something can be considered.

**Being-in-itself** (*Ansichsein*) is the Something taken **in distinction from** its relation to the Other — what it *is* simply by being itself, what would be true of it even if the relation to the Other were withdrawn. It is the Something's own side, turned inward, taken in what makes it *this* Something and not just a placeholder for the negation of an Other.

**Being-for-other** (*Sein-für-Anderes*) is the same Something taken **in** its relation to the Other — what it *is* by way of standing toward the Other, what would not be true of it apart from that standing.

Three clarifications are essential before going further, because each of them blocks a standard misreading.

**First, the German particles do not mean what their English glosses sometimes suggest.** *An sich* — "in itself" — does not mean "considered as it really is, behind appearance"; it does not mean "secretly," "essentially," or "as it would be in some privileged metaphysical inspection." It means, at this level, simply: *taken apart from its relation to the Other*. Likewise, *für Anderes* — "for other" — does not mean "as it appears to the Other," in the sense of how the Other perceives it. It means: *as it stands in the relation to the Other*. There is no observer in this picture. There is no appearance/reality distinction. Both terms are just labels for two sides of the same Something, considered as related-to or as not-related-to its Other. The richer Essence-level meanings of *Ansich* — appearance versus essence, phenomenal versus noumenal, the in-itself behind the for-us — belong to the Doctrine of Essence; they have not yet been earned.

**Second, the two sides do not correspond to two parts of the Something.** The Something does not have an "inside" and an "outside" the way a thing has a hidden interior and a visible surface; it is not made up of an *Ansichsein*-piece glued to a *Sein-für-Anderes*-piece. It is *one* Something, and the two sides are two aspects of the *same* whole, distinguished by whether we are attending to it as related-to-its-other or as taken-apart-from-that-relation.

**Third, the two sides are inseparable.** A Something that had only being-in-itself, with no being-for-other, would be a Something with no relation to any Other — and as §11 showed, that is no Something at all, since the very determinacy of a Something is constitutively its *not-being-the-other*. Conversely, a Something that had only being-for-other, with no being-in-itself, would be a Something whose entire content was its negative relation to an Other — and that, as §11's contradiction made clear, also collapses, since there would be nothing left to enter into the relation. Each side requires the other; neither can be subtracted without dissolving the Something.

What we have, then, is a structure already familiar in pattern: a Something that is *one* and yet contains within itself an opposition between two sides. *Dasein* had Reality and Negation as the two sides of its Quality (§11). Something has *being-in-itself* and *being-for-other* as the two sides of *its* unity. The pattern is recurring at a higher level. This is not coincidence; it is the *Logic*'s way of moving forward — each unity, once articulated, exhibits an internal opposition that is the seed of the next move.

## III. Anticipating Misreadings Again

The Doctrine of Essence will be where the *Ansich/Für-andres* distinction acquires its full philosophical weight, and many readers — under the influence of either Kant's noumenon/phenomenon or a vaguely realist intuition about hidden essences — import that weight backward into the present passage. It is worth resisting four such imports directly.

The first is the **noumenon/phenomenon misreading**: that being-in-itself is the Something "as it really is, in itself, behind appearances," while being-for-other is "the Something as it appears." This is Kant's vocabulary, not Hegel's at this stage. We are in the Doctrine of Being. There are no appearances yet; there is no subject to whom anything appears. *Ansichsein* and *Sein-für-Anderes* are *sides of the determinate*, not levels of access to it. Hegel will eventually use *Ansich/Für-andres* in a Kant-overlapping way much later in the Logic, but only after the appearance/essence distinction has itself been derived — and then with a deliberately critical inflection toward Kant. Here, the terms are simpler than Kant's; reading them as Kantian inflates them prematurely.

The second is the **private-self misreading**: that being-in-itself is what the Something is "to itself," in some quasi-subjective sense, while being-for-other is what it is "to the other." There is no self-consciousness here. The Something does not yet *have* a self in any reflective sense; it has only the minimal self-relation derived in §11 (the negation of the internal Negation, which is what made it a *one* at all). The "for" in *für Anderes* is not a relational predicate from one consciousness to another; it is the structural position of the Something *as standing in the relation to its Other*, with no perceiver on either side.

The third is the **hidden-essence misreading**: that being-in-itself is some deep nature that the Something *has* independently, which then *manifests itself* in its being-for-other. This is essence-talk, which the Doctrine of Being has not yet earned. At this level, being-in-itself is not a deeper layer; it is just the Something taken apart from its relation. There is no descent from a hidden inner to a visible outer — both sides are equally on the surface, in the precise sense that *Dasein* has no inside-vs-outside structure of that kind.

The fourth is the **relational-eliminativist misreading**: that since the Something is constitutively in relation to its Other, the talk of *being-in-itself* is empty — there is no "in itself" to speak of, and the Hegelian move is just a confused holdover from substance-thinking. This is the mirror image of the hidden-essence misreading, and it is just as wrong. The whole point of distinguishing the two sides is that *neither* side, taken alone, can sustain the Something. A purely relational Something is no Something; a purely isolated Something is no Something. The structure requires both, distinguished within the unity.

Avoid all four imports, and the two sides come back to their actual scope: simple, formal, sides of the same Something — *Ansichsein* the inward-turned face, *Sein-für-Anderes* the outward-facing face, both of one face.

## IV. From the Two Sides to Determination and Constitution

The two sides cannot remain merely named. As soon as we ask *what* the Something is in itself and *what* it is for the Other, the dialectic resumes — because each side, asked seriously, reveals that it cannot be cleanly separated from the other.

Ask: what is the Something *in itself*? The temptation is to answer "the Something is in itself just what it is, considered apart from the Other." But this answer is empty until we say what the "what it is" amounts to. And the "what it is" cannot be specified except by characterizing the Something — that is, by giving its determinations — which is to say, by saying what makes it *this* Something and not the Other. The moment we do that, we are already invoking the negative relation to the Other (since *this* and *not-the-other* are the same characterization). So the *in itself* side, when developed, contains *within* itself a reference to the Other — which it was supposed to be defined apart from.

Symmetrically: what is the Something *for the Other*? The temptation is "it is the side that stands toward the Other." But this side, asked what its content is, has to be *something* — it cannot stand toward the Other as pure relation with no content of its own — and what that something is comes from the Something's own determinacy, the side we just called the *in itself*.

So the two sides — supposed to be cleanly distinguished — each turn out to contain a reference to the other. They are not just inseparable; each, when developed, *passes into* the other. This is the dialectical engine at work again, one level up from §11.

Hegel articulates the resolution in two further determinations.

**Determination** (*Bestimmung*) is the Something's *in itself* — but explicitly *taken as such*, that is, with the recognition that it stands in opposition to the *for other*. Determination is what the Something is *meant to be*, what is constitutive of it, what carries over even as its standing toward the Other shifts. It is being-in-itself when it is *acknowledged* as one side of an internal opposition.

**Constitution** (*Beschaffenheit*) is the Something's *for other* — but likewise taken explicitly, as one side of the internal opposition. Constitution is the determinacy the Something *happens to have* in virtue of its standing toward the Other; it is the side of the Something that can shift with the relation, that varies, that can be otherwise.

The German word *Beschaffenheit* is hard to translate well — "constitution," "make-up," "way it happens to be." The key contrast with *Bestimmung* is that *Beschaffenheit* is *what the Something has* in its actual standing, while *Bestimmung* is *what the Something is to be*. The grammar of *to be* versus *to have* is the easiest way to feel the difference: a Something *is* its Determination (it *is* what it is meant to be, fundamentally), but it *has* its Constitution (it *has* a current standing that can be otherwise).

Two characterizations of the relation between these:

**They are inseparable in the same way the two sides were.** A Determination with no Constitution would be a *meant-to-be* with no actual standing — empty. A Constitution with no Determination would be an actual standing with no character that makes it *this* Something's standing rather than another's — equally empty. They require each other.

**But they can be in tension.** The Determination of a Something says what it is meant to be; the Constitution says what it actually has at present. These can diverge — a Something can have a Constitution that does not fully realize its Determination, or that goes beyond what its Determination strictly requires. This divergence is not yet the rich essence-level distinction between essence and appearance (we still have not earned that); at this stage it is just the elementary fact that *being-in-itself* and *being-for-other*, even after they have been more carefully named as *Determination* and *Constitution*, can fail to match.

This is the first place in the *Logic* where the Something's identity becomes precarious. If the Constitution diverges enough from the Determination, what is it that holds the Something together as *this* one? The question does not yet have an answer at this level; the answer (the Limit, finitude, and beyond) is what the next moves will give.

## V. The Limit

Hold the structure: a Something has a Determination and a Constitution; these are two sides of its unity; they require each other; they can be in tension; and they belong to *one* Something only because something keeps them anchored to it.

That something is the **Limit** (*Grenze*).

The Limit is not, here, a boundary in space (that is the limit of a geometric figure — a derivative meaning, much later in the Logic when Quality has passed into Quantity). The Limit is the determinate point at which the Something *ends* — at which what is the Something is no longer the Something, and is therefore the Other. The Limit is *what makes the Something this Something and not another*: it is the precise specification of where its determinacy ceases.

Three observations are essential.

**First, the Limit belongs to the Something.** The Limit is not external — it is not a boundary imposed on the Something by something outside it. It is internal to the Something, in the precise sense that *the Something is what it is* by virtue of being limited *in this way*. Drop the Limit and the Something has no determinacy left — it is back to indistinct Being. A Something *is* its Limit, in the sense that without that limit it is not *this* Something.

**Second, the Limit is shared with the Other.** This is the strange and important feature of the Limit. The point at which the Something ends is the same point at which the Other *begins*. A Limit is not a thing standing between Something and Other; it is the **single** determinate boundary that is at once *the Something's end* and *the Other's beginning*. So the Limit *belongs to both*. Without the Limit, neither Something nor Other would be the determinate it is; the same Limit constitutes them both.

**Third, the Limit is therefore *contradictory* in a precise way.** Considered as the Something's, the Limit is what the Something is (without it, no determinacy); considered as where the Something ends, the Limit is what the Something is *not* (it is precisely where the Something ceases to be). The Limit, then, is both *what the Something is* and *what it is not* — at once. This is not a wordplay; it is the structural fact that the Limit, *as* the Something's, is the Something's own *not-being-the-Other*, and as such it carries both the affirmative determinacy and its negation at the same point.

A more concrete way to feel this: think of the determinacy of any quality — color, say, in its abstract structure. *Red* has a determinacy that requires *not-orange* and *not-violet* at its edges. The "edge" where red stops being red is what makes red *red* (without the edge, the determinate quality would dissolve), and it is *also* where red ceases (where it becomes orange or violet). The same edge does both jobs — and it is for that reason that any determinate quality is, at its very borders, what it is *and* what it is not. (Note: this is illustration, not derivation; the dialectic does not depend on the color example. The point holds for any determinate quality whatever.)

## VI. From the Limit to Finitude

The Limit is the resolution of the Determination/Constitution tension: it anchors the Something. But the Limit, as we have just seen, is itself contradictory — it is both what the Something is and what it is not. A unity that holds within itself such a contradiction cannot rest.

What is the truth of a Something whose Limit is itself the place where the Something *both is and is not* itself? It is a Something whose determinacy *includes* its own ceasing. A Something that, by its very determinacy, is destined to come to an end. A Something that *has its end in itself*.

This is **Finitude** (*Endlichkeit*).

A finite Something is a Something whose Limit, taken seriously, is its end — not as a contingent fact that might or might not happen, but as something internal to what it is. Finitude is the Something's Limit *recognized as constitutive of the Something's very being*. Once recognized this way, the finite Something is no longer the static unity we started with; it carries its own perishing within it.

The next installment will develop this in full: the structure of finitude proper, the categories of *limit* and *ought* (*Schranke* and *Sollen*) as further articulations of finitude's internal contradiction, the way finitude immanently passes over into infinity, and the difference between *spurious* infinity (the endless repetition of finite-after-finite) and *true* infinity (the infinity that includes finitude as a moment of itself rather than excluding it). That arc — from finitude through spurious infinity to true infinity — closes the first major sub-circle of the Doctrine of Being. It is also the point at which, for the first time in the *Logic*, a determination is shown to be **infinite** — and the contemporary reader is most often startled by what "infinite" means at this stage of the exposition. The next installment will address that directly.

## VII. Method Observations at This Step

Three observations about the present step, each holding (with modifications) at later steps as well.

The first is that **what looked like two terms turned out to require four**. Being-in-itself and being-for-other were named first as the two sides of the Something. They could not stay merely named: as soon as we asked what each amounts to, each turned out to contain a reference to the other, and the two pairs had to be re-articulated as Determination (the in itself, taken explicitly as one side) and Constitution (the for other, taken explicitly as one side). The doubling — from two to four — is not arbitrary multiplication; it is what happens when two sides of a unity are pressed for their content. Expect this doubling to recur: any pair of sides held within a unity will, when pressed, articulate itself into a more developed structure.

The second is that **the resolving determination (the Limit) is structurally contradictory**. At each major step so far, the resolution of one contradiction is the seed of the next. Becoming resolved the Being/Nothing contradiction and was itself unstable; Something resolved the in/Negation contradiction within *Dasein* and was itself in tension with Other; now the Limit resolves the Determination/Constitution tension and is itself the bearer of a new contradiction (it is what the Something is *and* what it is not). The *Logic* does not arrive at static resolutions; it arrives at *more articulated* contradictions, each one carrying its own immanent movement. To follow the *Logic* is to expect this — and to feel the next move *not* as a defect of the current resolution, but as its natural unfolding.

The third is that **the move from Limit to Finitude is the first move in which the resolution is named as a property of the Something rather than as a relation between Something and Other**. Becoming was a structure across Being and Nothing; Other was a correlate of Something; the Limit was shared between Something and Other. Finitude, by contrast, is a determination *of the Something itself* — its determinacy as carrying its own end within it. This is a turn inward, an early foreshadowing of the much later turn toward Essence (in which all determinations become determinations of the Something taken as itself, with the Other internalized). It is too early to draw that arc explicitly here; but the careful reader will note that the *Logic*'s movement is not just one of refining horizontal categories but also one of progressively interiorizing what was at first external. The next installments will continue this turn.

## VIII. What Comes Next

The next installment will develop:

- **Finitude proper** — the Something as that which has its end in itself; the *Schranke* (limit-as-restriction) and the *Sollen* (the "ought" — the inner ferment by which the finite presses against its limit); the immanent contradiction of finitude that requires the move to infinity.
- **Spurious infinity** — the endless succession of finite-after-finite, the "bad infinity" of the understanding, and why it is not yet what Hegel means by *the* infinite.
- **True infinity** — the infinity that does not stand against the finite (which would make it itself a finite, since "infinity-vs-finitude" is a relation between two determinations and so a finite structure) but contains finitude as one of its own moments. This is one of the most original determinations in Hegel's *Logic* and one of the most commonly misread.

With true infinity, the first sub-circle of the Doctrine of Being closes. The path from there opens into **Being-for-self** (*Fürsichsein*) — the determination in which the Something has fully internalized its Other and stands as one — and from there to the dialectic of the **One and the Many**, which prepares the passage from Quality into Quantity.

---

## Coda

A reader who has followed this installment may have noticed that the *Logic* is now developing in pairs and quartets where it began in singles. *Being* and *Nothing*; then *Becoming* with its two moments; then *Dasein* with its Reality and Negation; then *Something* and *Other*; now *Determination* and *Constitution*, anchored by a Limit that is shared between Something and Other. The categories are accumulating their internal differentiations as we proceed. This is not embellishment. It is the *Logic*'s answer to the very first move we made — the move in which pure Being, the thinnest of all determinations, was shown to be unable to stand. Each step has been a step in the direction of *more determinacy*, of *more internal articulation*, of *more concreteness* in the technical Hegelian sense. The Limit and Finitude continue that direction. After the move into True Infinity and Being-for-self, the *Logic* will undertake its first major transition — out of Quality and into Quantity — and from that point on, the kind of categories at issue will shift. The patient reader who has followed this far has already done the harder work; what comes next is easier in pattern, even where it is unfamiliar in content.
